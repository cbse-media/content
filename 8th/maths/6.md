## Introduction

In this chapter, we bridge the gap between arithmetic and algebra. We start by observing how multiplication behaves when numbers are increased or decreased, leading us to the powerful **Distributive Property**.

This property is the foundation for Algebraic Identities—general formulas that hold true for any value. We will explore these identities not just through symbols, but through **geometric visualizations** (areas of squares and rectangles) and **number patterns**.

## Formula Sheet: Algebraic Identities

<Callout variant="tip">
**The Distributive Property:**
Multiplication distributes over addition.
$$ a(b + c) = ab + ac $$
</Callout>

### 1. Product of Two Binomials
$$ (a + m)(b + n) = ab + mb + an + mn $$

### 2. Square of a Sum (Identity 1A)
$$ (a + b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2 $$

### 3. Square of a Difference (Identity 1B)
$$ (a - b)^2 = a^2 - 2ab + b^2 $$

### 4. Difference of Squares (Identity 1C)
$$ (a + b)(a - b) = a^2 - b^2 $$

## Topics Covered
1.  **Distributive Property:** Understanding increments in products.
2.  **Geometric Proofs:** Visualizing $(a+b)^2$ and $(a-b)^2$ using areas.
3.  **Fast Multiplication:** Using identities to multiply numbers like $98 \times 102$ instantly.
4.  **Pattern Recognition:** Solving visual sequences using algebraic expressions.
5.  **Error Analysis:** Identifying common algebraic mistakes.

## Increments in Products

Consider the multiplication $23 \times 27$.
*   If we increase the first number (23) by 1, the new product is $24 \times 27$.
*   This effectively adds one more "set" of 27 to the total.
*   Increase = 27.

However, what if **both** numbers increase?
Let the numbers be $a$ and $b$. If we increase both by 1, we get $(a+1)(b+1)$.

$$
(a+1)(b+1) = ab + a + b + 1
$$

The product increases by the sum of the original numbers plus 1.

## Generalizing to Two Binomials

What happens if we multiply two sums?
$$ (a + m)(b + n) $$

We distribute each term in the first bracket to every term in the second bracket:
1.  Multiply $a$ by $b \rightarrow ab$
2.  Multiply $a$ by $n \rightarrow an$
3.  Multiply $m$ by $b \rightarrow mb$
4.  Multiply $m$ by $n \rightarrow mn$

$$ (a + m)(b + n) = ab + an + mb + mn $$

<Callout variant="tip">
**Key Insight:** This works for subtraction too! Just treat subtraction as adding a negative number.
$$(a - u)(b + v) = a(b+v) - u(b+v) = ab + av - ub - uv$$
</Callout>

## Identity 1B: Square of a Difference

$$ (a - b)^2 = a^2 - 2ab + b^2 $$

**Derivation:**
We can derive this from Identity 1A by replacing $b$ with $(-b)$:
$$ (a + (-b))^2 = a^2 + 2a(-b) + (-b)^2 $$
$$ = a^2 - 2ab + b^2 $$

## Identity 1C: Difference of Squares

This is a very useful pattern for fast calculation.
$$ (a + b)(a - b) = a^2 - b^2 $$

**Why does this work?**
Let's expand it using the distributive property:
$$ (a + b)(a - b) = a(a - b) + b(a - b) $$
$$ = a^2 - ab + ab - b^2 $$
$$ = a^2 - b^2 $$
*(The middle terms $-ab$ and $+ab$ cancel out!)*

<Callout variant="info">
**History Note:** The Indian mathematician **Sridharacharya (750 CE)** used a modified form of this identity: $a^2 = (a+b)(a-b) + b^2$ to calculate squares quickly.
</Callout>

## Multiplication by 11

Multiplying by 11 is easy because $11 = 10 + 1$.
$$ N \times 11 = N(10 + 1) = 10N + N $$

**Visual Trick:**
To multiply a number like 3874 by 11:
1.  Write down the last digit (4).
2.  Add pairs of adjacent digits from right to left.
3.  Write down the first digit (plus any carry).

**Example: $3874 \times 11$**
*   Last digit: **4**
*   $7+4 = 11$ (Write **1**, Carry 1)
*   $8+7+1(\text{carry}) = 16$ (Write **6**, Carry 1)
*   $3+8+1(\text{carry}) = 12$ (Write **2**, Carry 1)
*   First digit: $3+1(\text{carry}) = 4$
*   **Result: 42614**

## Using Difference of Squares

Calculate $45 \times 55$.
Notice that these numbers are centered around 50.
$$ 45 = 50 - 5 $$
$$ 55 = 50 + 5 $$

Using Identity 1C:
$$ (50 - 5)(50 + 5) = 50^2 - 5^2 $$
$$ = 2500 - 25 $$
$$ = 2475 $$

## Calculating Squares near Base

Calculate $99^2$.
$$ 99 = 100 - 1 $$
$$ (100 - 1)^2 = 100^2 - 2(100)(1) + 1^2 $$
$$ = 10000 - 200 + 1 $$
$$ = 9801 $$

## Triangular Patterns

Consider a pattern of circles arranged in triangles or squares.
If we examine the number of items in each step, we can often find a quadratic formula ($an^2 + bn + c$).

**Example:**
*   Step 1: 3 items
*   Step 2: 8 items
*   Step 3: 15 items

Let's look at the structure.
*   $3 = 1 \times 3$
*   $8 = 2 \times 4$
*   $15 = 3 \times 5$

The pattern is $k \times (k+2)$.
Expression: $k^2 + 2k$.

## Sum of Squares Pattern

$$ 2(a^2 + b^2) = (a+b)^2 + (a-b)^2 $$

This identity connects the sum of squares to the square of sum and square of difference.

**Verification:**
RHS $= (a^2 + 2ab + b^2) + (a^2 - 2ab + b^2)$
$= 2a^2 + 2b^2$
$= 2(a^2 + b^2)$ = LHS.

<Callout variant="success">
**Application:** If you know the sum $(a+b)$ and the difference $(a-b)$, you can easily find the sum of squares without knowing the numbers themselves!
</Callout>

## 1. Which is greater: $(a-b)^2$ or $(b-a)^2$?
**Answer:** They are equal.
Proof: $(b-a) = -(a-b)$. Squaring a negative number makes it positive.
$$ (b-a)^2 = [-(a-b)]^2 = (a-b)^2 $$

## 2. Express 100 as difference of two squares
We need $a^2 - b^2 = 100 \implies (a+b)(a-b) = 100$.
Factors of 100 are: $(1,100), (2,50), (4,25), (5,20), (10,10)$.
For $a$ and $b$ to be integers, factors must have the same parity (both even or both odd).
*   Pair $(2, 50)$:
    *   $a+b = 50, a-b = 2 \implies 2a = 52 \implies a=26, b=24$.
    *   $26^2 - 24^2 = 676 - 576 = 100$.
*   Pair $(10, 10)$:
    *   $a=10, b=0$. $10^2 - 0^2 = 100$.

## 3. Calculating Squares
**$406^2$**
$$ (400+6)^2 = 160000 + 36 + 4800 = 164836 $$

**$72^2$**
$$ (70+2)^2 = 4900 + 4 + 280 = 5184 $$

**$1097^2$**
$$ (1100-3)^2 = 1210000 + 9 - 6600 = 1203409 $$

**$124^2$**
$$ (125-1)^2 = 15625 + 1 - 250 = 15376 $$

## Area of Dashed Region
The figure shows a rectangle with a corner removed (L-shape).
Let outer dimensions be $p$ and $s+r$.
Inner cutout dimensions are $p-r$ and $s$.

**Method 1 (Subtraction):**
Total Area - Cutout Area
$$ p(s+r) - s(p-r) $$
$$ = ps + pr - sp + sr $$
$$ = pr + sr = r(p+s) $$

**Method 2 (Addition of Rectangles):**
Split into bottom rectangle ($s \times r$) and side rectangle ($p \times r$).
$$ sr + pr = r(s+p) $$

**Calculation:**
$p=6, r=3.5, s=9$.
Area $= 3.5(6 + 9) = 3.5 \times 15 = 52.5$.

## Example 1: Mind the Mistake
**Incorrect Expansion:** $(a + 3)^2 = a^2 + 9$
**Explanation:** This is a common error where the middle term is forgotten.
**Correct:**
$$ (a + 3)^2 = a^2 + 2(a)(3) + 3^2 = a^2 + 6a + 9 $$

## Example 2: Negative Distributivity
**Expand:** $-3x(2y - 5)$
$$ = (-3x)(2y) + (-3x)(-5) $$
$$ = -6xy + 15x $$

## Example 3: Fractions
**Simplify:** $(x + \frac{1}{x})^2$
Using Identity 1A:
$$ x^2 + 2(x)(\frac{1}{x}) + (\frac{1}{x})^2 $$
$$ = x^2 + 2 + \frac{1}{x^2} $$

## Example 4: Large Numbers
**Calculate:** $53 \times 47$
Recognize this as $(50+3)(50-3)$.
$$ 50^2 - 3^2 $$
$$ = 2500 - 9 = 2491 $$

## The Problem
You have a triangle of 10 coins (pointing up). You need to invert it (point down) by moving the minimum number of coins.

## Solution Logic

1.  **Triangle T3 (6 coins):**
    *   Move the 3 corners? No.
    *   Move just 2 coins.
    *   Move the top coin to the bottom center.
    *   Move two bottom corners up.

2.  **Triangle T4 (10 coins):**
    *   The triangle has 4 rows (1, 2, 3, 4).
    *   To invert it, we basically need to remove the "corners" that define the shape and place them on the opposite side.
    *   **Minimum Moves:** **3**.
    *   **How:**
        1.  Move the top tip coin to the very bottom (below the row of 4).
        2.  Move the two coins at the ends of the bottom row (row 4) to the top positions (forming the new base of the inverted triangle).

## General Formula
For a triangle with side length $n$:
$$ \text{Moves} = \lfloor \frac{n(n+1)}{6} \rfloor $$

*   $n=3$ (6 coins): $\lfloor 12/6 \rfloor = 2$.
*   $n=4$ (10 coins): $\lfloor 20/6 \rfloor = 3$.
*   $n=5$ (15 coins): $\lfloor 30/6 \rfloor = 5$.
