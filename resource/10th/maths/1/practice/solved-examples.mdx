## Example 1
**Question:** Consider the numbers $4^n$, where $n$ is a natural number. Check whether there is any value of $n$ for which $4^n$ ends with the digit zero.

**Solution:**
If the number $4^n$, for any $n$, were to end with the digit zero, it would be divisible by $5$. This means the prime factorization of $4^n$ would contain the prime number $5$.

Let's look at the prime factorization of $4^n$:
$$
4^n = (2^2)^n = 2^{2n}
$$

The only prime in the factorization of $4^n$ is $2$. The uniqueness of the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic guarantees that there are no other primes in its factorization. Since $5$ is not present, there is no natural number $n$ for which $4^n$ ends with the digit zero.

## Example 2
**Question:** Find the LCM and HCF of 6 and 20 by the prime factorization method.

**Solution:**
First, write the prime factorization of both numbers:
$$
6 = 2^1 \times 3^1
$$
$$
20 = 2 \times 2 \times 5 = 2^2 \times 5^1
$$

*   **HCF:** Product of the smallest power of each common prime factor. The only common prime is 2, and the smallest power is $2^1$.
    $$ \text{HCF}(6, 20) = 2^1 = 2 $$

*   **LCM:** Product of the greatest power of each prime factor involved.
    $$ \text{LCM}(6, 20) = 2^2 \times 3^1 \times 5^1 = 4 \times 3 \times 5 = 60 $$

## Example 3
**Question:** Find the HCF of 96 and 404 by the prime factorization method. Hence, find their LCM.

**Solution:**
Prime factorization:
$$
96 = 2^5 \times 3
$$
$$
404 = 2^2 \times 101
$$

The HCF is the product of the smallest power of the common prime factors:
$$
\text{HCF}(96, 404) = 2^2 = 4
$$

Using the formula $\text{HCF}(a,b) \times \text{LCM}(a,b) = a \times b$:
$$
\text{LCM}(96, 404) = \frac{96 \times 404}{\text{HCF}(96, 404)} = \frac{96 \times 404}{4} = 96 \times 101 = 9696
$$

## Example 4
**Question:** Find the HCF and LCM of 6, 72 and 120, using the prime factorization method.

**Solution:**
Prime factorization:
$$
6 = 2 \times 3
$$
$$
72 = 2^3 \times 3^2
$$
$$
120 = 2^3 \times 3 \times 5
$$

*   **HCF:** Smallest powers of common factors ($2$ and $3$).
    $$ \text{HCF}(6, 72, 120) = 2^1 \times 3^1 = 6 $$

*   **LCM:** Greatest powers of all prime factors involved ($2, 3,$ and $5$).
    $$ \text{LCM}(6, 72, 120) = 2^3 \times 3^2 \times 5^1 = 8 \times 9 \times 5 = 360 $$

## Example 5
**Question:** Prove that $\sqrt{3}$ is irrational.

**Solution:**
Let us assume, to the contrary, that $\sqrt{3}$ is rational. We can find coprime integers $a$ and $b$ ($b \neq 0$) such that:
$$
\sqrt{3} = \frac{a}{b}
$$

Rearranging and squaring both sides:
$$
b\sqrt{3} = a \implies 3b^2 = a^2
$$

Therefore, $a^2$ is divisible by $3$. By Theorem 1.2, $a$ is also divisible by $3$.
Let $a = 3c$ for some integer $c$. Substitute this back:
$$
3b^2 = (3c)^2 \implies 3b^2 = 9c^2 \implies b^2 = 3c^2
$$

This means $b^2$ is divisible by $3$, and so $b$ is also divisible by $3$.
Thus, $a$ and $b$ have at least $3$ as a common factor, contradicting the fact that they are coprime.
Our assumption was wrong. Therefore, $\sqrt{3}$ is irrational.

## Example 6
**Question:** Show that $5 - \sqrt{3}$ is irrational.

**Solution:**
Let us assume, to the contrary, that $5 - \sqrt{3}$ is rational. 
We can find coprime integers $a$ and $b$ ($b \neq 0$) such that:
$$
5 - \sqrt{3} = \frac{a}{b}
$$

Rearranging the equation:
$$
\sqrt{3} = 5 - \frac{a}{b} = \frac{5b - a}{b}
$$

Since $a$ and $b$ are integers, $\frac{5b - a}{b}$ is a rational number. This would mean $\sqrt{3}$ is rational.
However, this contradicts the proven fact that $\sqrt{3}$ is irrational. 
The contradiction arises from our incorrect assumption.
Therefore, $5 - \sqrt{3}$ is irrational.

## Example 7
**Question:** Show that $3\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.

**Solution:**
Let us assume, to the contrary, that $3\sqrt{2}$ is rational.
We can find coprime integers $a$ and $b$ ($b \neq 0$) such that:
$$
3\sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b}
$$

Rearranging the equation:
$$
\sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{3b}
$$

Since $3, a,$ and $b$ are integers, $\frac{a}{3b}$ is a rational number, which implies $\sqrt{2}$ is rational.
But this contradicts the fact that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.
Therefore, we conclude that $3\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.
