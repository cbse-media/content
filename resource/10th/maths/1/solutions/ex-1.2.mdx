## Question 1
**Prove that $\sqrt{5}$ is irrational.**

**Solution:**
We will prove this using the method of contradiction.
Let us assume, to the contrary, that $\sqrt{5}$ is rational.
Then, we can find two coprime integers $a$ and $b$ ($b \neq 0$) such that:
$$
\sqrt{5} = \frac{a}{b}
$$

Rearranging the equation, we get:
$$
b\sqrt{5} = a
$$

Squaring both sides:
$$
5b^2 = a^2 \quad \text{--- (Equation 1)}
$$

This implies that $a^2$ is divisible by $5$.
According to Theorem 1.2, if a prime number divides $a^2$, it also divides $a$. Thus, $a$ is divisible by $5$.

Let $a = 5c$ for some integer $c$. Substituting this value of $a$ into Equation 1:
$$
5b^2 = (5c)^2
$$
$$
5b^2 = 25c^2
$$
$$
b^2 = 5c^2
$$

This implies that $b^2$ is divisible by $5$, and consequently, $b$ is also divisible by $5$.
Therefore, both $a$ and $b$ have $5$ as a common factor.
But this contradicts our initial assumption that $a$ and $b$ are coprime (having no common factor other than 1).

This contradiction has arisen because of our incorrect assumption that $\sqrt{5}$ is rational.
Hence, **$\sqrt{5}$ is irrational**.

## Question 2
**Prove that $3 + 2\sqrt{5}$ is irrational.**

**Solution:**
Let us assume, to the contrary, that $3 + 2\sqrt{5}$ is rational.
This means we can find coprime integers $a$ and $b$ ($b \neq 0$) such that:
$$
3 + 2\sqrt{5} = \frac{a}{b}
$$

Rearranging the terms to isolate $\sqrt{5}$:
$$
2\sqrt{5} = \frac{a}{b} - 3
$$
$$
2\sqrt{5} = \frac{a - 3b}{b}
$$
$$
\sqrt{5} = \frac{a - 3b}{2b}
$$

Since $a$ and $b$ are integers, the expression $\frac{a - 3b}{2b}$ evaluates to a rational number.
This implies that $\sqrt{5}$ is rational.
However, we already know that $\sqrt{5}$ is an irrational number.
This contradicts the fact that $\sqrt{5}$ is irrational. 
The contradiction arose from our incorrect assumption.
Hence, **$3 + 2\sqrt{5}$ is irrational**.

## Question 3
**Prove that the following are irrationals:**
(i) $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$
(ii) $7\sqrt{5}$
(iii) $6 + \sqrt{2}$

**Solutions:**

**(i) Prove $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ is irrational:**
Let us assume, to the contrary, that $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ is rational.
Thus, we can find coprime integers $a$ and $b$ ($b \neq 0, a \neq 0$) such that:
$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{a}{b}
$$
Taking the reciprocal of both sides:
$$
\sqrt{2} = \frac{b}{a}
$$
Since $a$ and $b$ are integers, $\frac{b}{a}$ is a rational number. This implies that $\sqrt{2}$ is rational.
But we know that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational. This contradiction shows our assumption is wrong.
Hence, **$\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$ is irrational**.

**(ii) Prove $7\sqrt{5}$ is irrational:**
Let us assume, to the contrary, that $7\sqrt{5}$ is rational.
We can find coprime integers $a$ and $b$ ($b \neq 0$) such that:
$$
7\sqrt{5} = \frac{a}{b}
$$
Rearranging to isolate $\sqrt{5}$:
$$
\sqrt{5} = \frac{a}{7b}
$$
Since $7, a,$ and $b$ are integers, $\frac{a}{7b}$ is a rational number. This means $\sqrt{5}$ is rational.
This contradicts the fact that $\sqrt{5}$ is irrational.
Hence, **$7\sqrt{5}$ is irrational**.

**(iii) Prove $6 + \sqrt{2}$ is irrational:**
Let us assume, to the contrary, that $6 + \sqrt{2}$ is rational.
We can find coprime integers $a$ and $b$ ($b \neq 0$) such that:
$$
6 + \sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b}
$$
Rearranging to isolate $\sqrt{2}$:
$$
\sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b} - 6
$$
$$
\sqrt{2} = \frac{a - 6b}{b}
$$
Since $a$ and $b$ are integers, $\frac{a - 6b}{b}$ is a rational number, which implies $\sqrt{2}$ is rational.
But this contradicts the fact that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.
Hence, **$6 + \sqrt{2}$ is irrational**.
