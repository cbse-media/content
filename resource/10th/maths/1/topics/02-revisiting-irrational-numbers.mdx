## What is an Irrational Number?

A number $s$ is called **irrational** if it cannot be written in the form $\frac{p}{q}$, where $p$ and $q$ are integers and $q \neq 0$. 

Examples of irrational numbers include $\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, \sqrt{15}, \pi,$ and non-terminating, non-repeating decimals like $0.101101110\dots$

In this section, we will formally prove that numbers like $\sqrt{2}$ and $\sqrt{3}$ are irrational. To do this, we need an important theorem derived from the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic.

## A Crucial Theorem

<Callout variant="warning">
**Theorem 1.2:** Let $p$ be a prime number. If $p$ divides $a^2$, then $p$ divides $a$, where $a$ is a positive integer.
</Callout>

**Proof Concept:**
If we write the prime factorization of $a$ as $a = p_1 p_2 \dots p_n$, then $a^2 = p_1^2 p_2^2 \dots p_n^2$.
If $p$ divides $a^2$, $p$ must be one of the prime factors of $a^2$. Because prime factorization is unique, $p$ must be one of the primes $p_1, p_2, \dots, p_n$. Therefore, $p$ must also divide $a$.

## Proving $\sqrt{2}$ is Irrational

We use a technique called **proof by contradiction**.

**Theorem 1.3:** $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.

**Proof:**
1. Let us assume, to the contrary, that $\sqrt{2}$ is **rational**.
2. This means we can find integers $r$ and $s$ ($s \neq 0$) such that $\sqrt{2} = \frac{r}{s}$.
3. Suppose $r$ and $s$ have a common factor other than 1. We divide by this common factor to get $\sqrt{2} = \frac{a}{b}$, where $a$ and $b$ are **coprime** (they have no common factor other than 1).
4. Rearranging, we get:
   $$b\sqrt{2} = a$$
5. Squaring both sides:
   $$2b^2 = a^2$$
6. This means $2$ divides $a^2$. By Theorem 1.2, it follows that **$2$ divides $a$**.
7. We can write $a = 2c$ for some integer $c$. Substituting this back into the equation:
   $$2b^2 = (2c)^2$$
   $$2b^2 = 4c^2$$
   $$b^2 = 2c^2$$
8. This means $2$ divides $b^2$, and so **$2$ divides $b$**.
9. We have found that $a$ and $b$ have at least $2$ as a common factor.
10. But this contradicts our initial assumption that $a$ and $b$ are coprime.
11. This contradiction has arisen because of our incorrect assumption that $\sqrt{2}$ is rational.

Therefore, we conclude that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.

## Operations with Irrationals

From earlier classes, remember these properties:
* The sum or difference of a rational and an irrational number is **irrational**.
* The product and quotient of a non-zero rational and an irrational number is **irrational**.
