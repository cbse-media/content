## The Angle Sum Property

What is the sum of the three interior angles of a triangle?
Let's prove it using what we learned about parallel lines!

Imagine a triangle $ABC$. We want to find $\angle A + \angle B + \angle C$.

**The Proof:**
1.  Draw a straight line $XY$ passing through the top vertex $A$, such that $XY$ is completely parallel to the base $BC$.
2.  Because $XY \parallel BC$, the line $AB$ acts as a transversal. 
    Therefore, the alternate interior angles are equal: **$\angle XAB = \angle B$**.
3.  Similarly, the line $AC$ acts as a transversal.
    Therefore, the alternate interior angles are equal: **$\angle YAC = \angle C$**.
4.  Look at vertex $A$. The angles $\angle XAB$, $\angle BAC$ (which is $\angle A$), and $\angle YAC$ sit together on the straight line $XY$.
    Therefore, they must add up to $180^\circ$:
    $$ \angle XAB + \angle BAC + \angle YAC = 180^\circ $$
5.  Substitute the alternate angles we found in steps 2 and 3:
    $$ \angle B + \angle A + \angle C = 180^\circ $$

<Callout variant="tip">
**Angle Sum Property:**
The sum of the three interior angles in *any* triangle is always exactly **$180^\circ$**.
</Callout>

### The Exterior Angle Theorem

How is the exterior angle related to the interior angles?
1.  We know $\angle ACB$ and $\angle ACD$ form a linear pair (a straight line):
    $$ \angle ACB + \angle ACD = 180^\circ $$
2.  We also know the sum of the interior angles of the triangle is $180^\circ$:
    $$ \angle A + \angle B + \angle ACB = 180^\circ $$
3.  Since both equations equal $180^\circ$, we can set them equal to each other:
    $$ \angle ACB + \angle ACD = \angle A + \angle B + \angle ACB $$
4.  Subtract $\angle ACB$ from both sides, and we are left with:
    $$ \angle ACD = \angle A + \angle B $$

**Rule:** The measure of an exterior angle of a triangle is exactly equal to the sum of the **two opposite interior angles**.
