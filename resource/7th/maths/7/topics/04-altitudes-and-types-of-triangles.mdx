## The Altitude (Height) of a Triangle

When we talk about the "height" of a person or a building, we measure straight up from the ground at a $90^\circ$ angle. We do the exact same thing for triangles.

An **altitude** of a triangle is a line segment drawn from a vertex that is perfectly **perpendicular ($90^\circ$)** to the opposite side (the base). 

*   Every triangle has **three** altitudes, one from each vertex.
*   In an **obtuse triangle**, the altitude might actually drop *outside* the triangle! You have to extend the base line outwards to meet the perpendicular drop.

### Right-Angled Triangles
Does there exist a triangle in which a *side* is also an altitude? 
Yes! In a **right-angled triangle**, the two sides that form the $90^\circ$ angle act as altitudes for each other. 

## Types of Triangles (By Angles)

We previously classified triangles by their sides (Equilateral, Isosceles, Scalene). We can also classify them by their internal angles:

1.  **Acute-angled Triangle:** A triangle where **all three** internal angles are acute (less than $90^\circ$).
2.  **Right-angled Triangle:** A triangle with exactly **one** right angle ($90^\circ$). (The other two must be acute and add up to $90^\circ$).
3.  **Obtuse-angled Triangle:** A triangle with exactly **one** obtuse angle (greater than $90^\circ$). (The other two must be acute).

<Callout variant="warning">
**Can a triangle have two right angles?**
No! If a triangle had two $90^\circ$ angles, their sum would already be $180^\circ$. The third angle would have to be $0^\circ$, meaning the lines are parallel and will never meet to form a closed triangle!
</Callout>
