### 1. Corresponding Angles
Angles that are in the same relative position at each intersection.
*   **Pairs:** ($\angle 1$ and $\angle 5$), ($\angle 2$ and $\angle 6$), ($\angle 4$ and $\angle 8$), ($\angle 3$ and $\angle 7$)
*   **Rule:** When lines are parallel, corresponding angles are **equal**. ($\angle 1 = \angle 5$)

### 2. Alternate Interior Angles
Angles that are on opposite sides of the transversal and *between* the two lines.
*   **Pairs:** ($\angle 3$ and $\angle 5$), ($\angle 4$ and $\angle 6$)
*   **Rule:** When lines are parallel, alternate interior angles are **equal**. ($\angle 3 = \angle 5$)

### 3. Alternate Exterior Angles
Angles that are on opposite sides of the transversal and *outside* the two parallel lines.
*   **Pairs:** ($\angle 1$ and $\angle 7$), ($\angle 2$ and $\angle 8$)
*   **Rule:** When lines are parallel, alternate exterior angles are **equal**. ($\angle 1 = \angle 7$)

### 4. Interior Angles on the Same Side
Angles that are on the *same side* of the transversal and *between* the two lines. These are also known as Consecutive Interior Angles.
*   **Pairs:** ($\angle 4$ and $\angle 5$), ($\angle 3$ and $\angle 6$)
*   **Rule:** When lines are parallel, interior angles on the same side add up to $180^\circ$. They are **supplementary**. ($\angle 4 + \angle 5 = 180^\circ$)

<Callout variant="warning">
**The "If and Only If" Condition**
If two lines are intersected by a transversal, and *any* pair of corresponding angles are equal, then the lines **must be parallel**. This works backwards too! If you measure the angles and they aren't perfectly equal, the lines are not parallel and will eventually intersect.
</Callout>
