## Intersecting Lines

When a pair of lines meet each other at a single point on a plane surface, we say that the lines **intersect** each other. When two lines intersect, they form exactly **four angles** around the point of intersection.

### Linear Pairs
Adjacent angles (angles that are side-by-side) formed by two intersecting lines form a straight line. Because a straight line measures $180^\circ$, these adjacent angles always add up to $180^\circ$. They are called **linear pairs**.
$$ \angle a + \angle b = 180^\circ $$
$$ \angle b + \angle c = 180^\circ $$

### Vertically Opposite Angles
Opposite angles formed by two intersecting lines are called **vertically opposite angles**. 
Because $\angle a + \angle b = 180^\circ$ and $\angle b + \angle c = 180^\circ$, it mathematically forces $\angle a$ to be exactly equal to $\angle c$.
<Callout variant="tip">
**Theorem:** Vertically opposite angles are always equal to each other.
$$ \angle a = \angle c $$
$$ \angle b = \angle d $$
</Callout>

## Perpendicular Lines

Can you draw a pair of intersecting lines such that all four angles are equal?
Since all four angles must add up to $360^\circ$, each angle would be $360^\circ \div 4 = 90^\circ$.

**Perpendicular lines** are a pair of lines which intersect each other exactly at right angles ($90^\circ$). In geometry, we often denote a $90^\circ$ angle with a small square symbol at the vertex instead of drawing a curved arc.
