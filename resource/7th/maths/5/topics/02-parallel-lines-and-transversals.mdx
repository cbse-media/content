## Parallel Lines

Look at the opposite edges of a ruler, or the metal tracks of a railway line. They maintain a constant distance apart and do not seem likely to intersect each other, no matter how far they are extended. 

<Callout variant="info">
**Definition:** **Parallel lines** are a pair of lines that lie on the same plane and do not meet, however far we extend them at both ends.
</Callout>

In mathematics, we use arrow marks (`>`) on the lines to visually indicate that a set of lines is parallel to each other.

## Transversals

When a single line slices across a pair of lines, we give it a special name.

<Callout variant="info">
**Definition:** A line that intersects two or more lines at distinct points is called a **transversal**.
</Callout>

Notice that when a transversal intersects two lines, it creates exactly **8 angles**.

Even if the lines $l$ and $m$ are completely random and not parallel, these 8 angles are always formed. In the next section, we will see the magical relationships that happen when lines $l$ and $m$ *are* parallel!
