## Introduction

Look around you: the edges of a tabletop, the lines on a piece of ruled paper, the grills on a window, or the tracks of a railway line. Lines are everywhere! In this chapter, we will explore the mathematical relationships between lines on a flat (plane) surface. 

We will study what happens when lines cross each other (**intersecting lines**), when they cross exactly at right angles (**perpendicular lines**), and when they never cross no matter how far they are extended (**parallel lines**). Furthermore, we will unlock the secrets of the angles formed when a single line slices across a pair of lines (**transversals**).

<Callout variant="info">
**Did you know?**
In geometry, we study "ideal" lines. An ideal line has infinite length and zero thickness. Even though we can't draw a line without thickness in real life, reasoning about ideal lines helps us design strong buildings, bridges, and precise engineering tools!
</Callout>

## Quick Reference: Key Angle Rules

Memorize these fundamental angle properties:

### 1. Intersecting Lines
*   **Linear Pairs:** Adjacent angles formed by intersecting lines add up to **$180^\circ$**. (e.g., $\angle a + \angle b = 180^\circ$).
*   **Vertically Opposite Angles:** The opposite angles formed by intersecting lines are always **equal**. (e.g., $\angle a = \angle c$).

### 2. Parallel Lines and Transversals
When a transversal intersects two **parallel** lines:
*   **Corresponding Angles** are **equal**.
*   **Alternate Interior Angles** are **equal**.
*   **Alternate Exterior Angles** are **equal**.
*   **Interior Angles on the same side** of the transversal are **supplementary** (they add up to $180^\circ$).

<Callout variant="tip">
**The Golden Rule:**
If a transversal intersects two lines and *any one* of the above parallel conditions is met (e.g., corresponding angles are equal), then the two lines **must be parallel** to each other!
</Callout>
