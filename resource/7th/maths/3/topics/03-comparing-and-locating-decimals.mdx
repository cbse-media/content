## The Zero Dilemma!

Is $0.2$ the same as $0.20$ or $0.200$? 
Some people think that putting zeros on the right side alters the value of the decimal number. Let's use place value to find out.

| Decimal Number | Units | Tenths | Hundredths | Thousandths |
| :--- | :---: | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| $0.2$ | $0$ | $2$ | | |
| $0.20$ | $0$ | $2$ | $0$ | |
| $0.200$ | $0$ | $2$ | $0$ | $0$ |

**Conclusion:** We can see that $0.2$, $0.20$, and $0.200$ represent the exact same quantity: $2$ tenths. Adding trailing zeros to the right of a decimal fraction does NOT change its value!
*(Note: However, $0.2$, $0.02$, and $0.002$ are drastically different!)*

## Comparing Decimals: Which is Larger?

Which is larger: $6.456$ or $6.465$?

To compare decimals, we follow a simple rule: **Start from the most significant digit (the leftmost digit) and compare moving right.**

1.  **Compare Units:** Both numbers have a $6$ in the units place. ($6 = 6$)
2.  **Compare Tenths:** Both numbers have a $4$ in the tenths place. ($4 = 4$)
3.  **Compare Hundredths:** The first number has a $5$, but the second number has a $6$.
4.  Since $6 > 5$, we know instantly that **$6.465 > 6.456$**.

<Callout variant="tip">
**Why can we stop comparing?**
Once we find a difference in a larger place value (like hundredths), it doesn't matter what digits come after it (like thousandths). Even if the first number had a $9$ in the thousandths place and the second had a $0$, the hundredths place difference is far more powerful!
</Callout>

### Example
Which is greater: $3.81$ or $13.800$?
*   Look at the whole numbers first! The first number has $3$ units, the second has $13$ units. 
*   Since $13 > 3$, **$13.800 > 3.81$**. We don't even need to look at the decimal parts.
