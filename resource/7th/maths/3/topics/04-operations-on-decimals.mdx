## Addition and Subtraction

Adding and subtracting decimals uses the exact same standard procedure as whole numbers. The most critical rule is to **always align the decimal points** before performing the operation. This ensures that you are adding tenths to tenths, hundredths to hundredths, etc.

### Example 1: Addition
Priya requires $2.7 \text{ m}$ of cloth for her skirt, and Shylaja requires $3.5 \text{ m}$ for her kurti. What is the total quantity of cloth needed?

**Step-by-step:**
$$ \begin{array}{r@{\quad}l} 2.7 & \\ + 3.5 & \\ \hline \end{array} $$
1. Add the tenths: $7 + 5 = 12$ tenths. 
2. $12$ tenths is $1$ whole unit and $2$ tenths. Write down $2$, carry over $1$ to the units column.
3. Add the units: $2 + 3 + 1 \text{ (carried)} = 6$.
$$ \begin{array}{r@{\quad}l} \color{red}{^1} 2.7 & \\ + 3.5 & \\ \hline \mathbf{6.2} & \text{m} \end{array} $$

### Example 2: Subtraction
How much longer is Shylaja’s cloth compared to Priya’s? ($3.5 \text{ m} - 2.7 \text{ m}$)

**Step-by-step:**
$$ \begin{array}{r@{\quad}l} 3.5 & \\ - 2.7 & \\ \hline \end{array} $$
1. Subtract the tenths: We cannot subtract $7$ from $5$.
2. Borrow $1$ unit from the $3$. $1$ unit becomes $10$ tenths. Now we have $15$ tenths.
3. $15 - 7 = 8$ tenths.
4. Subtract the units: $2 - 2 = 0$.
$$ \begin{array}{r@{\quad}l} \color{red}{^2} \cancel{3}. \color{red}{^{15}} \cancel{5} & \\ - 2.7 & \\ \hline \mathbf{0.8} & \text{m} \end{array} $$

<Callout variant="warning">
**Filling in the Blanks!**
When adding or subtracting numbers with different decimal lengths (like $5.6$ and $2.15$), you can add a `0` to the end of the shorter decimal to help you align them visually!
$5.60 - 2.15$ is much easier to read than $5.6 - 2.15$.
</Callout>

## Estimating Sums and Differences

Estimating the result *before* computing can help you catch mistakes (like putting the decimal point in the wrong place!).

**Claim:** "If we add two decimal numbers, the sum will always be greater than the sum of their whole number parts. Also, the sum will always be less than $2$ more than the sum of their whole number parts."

**Let's test this:** Add $25.936$ and $8.202$.
1.  **Lower Bound:** Add the whole numbers only: $25 + 8 = 33$. The true sum must be *greater* than 33 because we are also adding the fractional parts ($0.936 + 0.202$).
2.  **Upper Bound:** The fractional parts are both less than $1$. Therefore, their combined sum must be less than $2$. So the total sum must be less than $33 + 2 = 35$.
3.  **Conclusion:** The exact answer must be between $33$ and $35$. 

*(Actual sum: $34.138$, which perfectly fits our estimation!)*
