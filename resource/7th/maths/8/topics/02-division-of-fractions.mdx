## Converting Division to Multiplication

What does $12 \div 4$ mean? It asks: "What should be multiplied by 4 to get 12?"
We can use this exact same logic to divide fractions!

What is $1 \div \frac{2}{3}$? 
Rewritten as multiplication: 
$$ \frac{2}{3} \times ? = 1 $$

What should be multiplied by $\frac{2}{3}$ to get $1$? We need a fraction that cancels out both the $2$ and the $3$. That fraction is $\frac{3}{2}$.
Therefore, $1 \div \frac{2}{3} = \frac{3}{2}$.

## The Reciprocal

<Callout variant="info">
**Definition:** The **reciprocal** of a fraction is formed by swapping its numerator and denominator. When you multiply a fraction by its reciprocal, the product is always $1$.
*Example:* The reciprocal of $\frac{3}{5}$ is $\frac{5}{3}$.
</Callout>

## The Rule for Dividing Fractions

Using reciprocals, Brahmagupta and Bhaskara II clarified a simple rule for dividing fractions:
> *"Division of one fraction by another is equivalent to multiplication of the first fraction by the reciprocal of the second."*

**Formula:**
$$ \frac{a}{b} \div \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a}{b} \times \frac{d}{c} = \frac{a \times d}{b \times c} $$

**Example:** Calculate $\frac{2}{3} \div \frac{3}{5}$
1. Keep the first fraction: $\frac{2}{3}$
2. Change $\div$ to $\times$
3. Flip the second fraction to its reciprocal: $\frac{5}{3}$
4. Multiply: $\frac{2}{3} \times \frac{5}{3} = \mathbf{\frac{10}{9}}$

## Dividend, Divisor, and Quotient

In a division problem: $\text{Dividend} \div \text{Divisor} = \text{Quotient}$.

When dealing with whole numbers, the quotient is usually smaller than the dividend (e.g., $6 \div 3 = 2$). But with fractions, something interesting happens:
*   What is $6 \div \frac{1}{4}$?
*   $6 \times \frac{4}{1} = \mathbf{24}$.

**The Rule:** 
When the divisor is a proper fraction (between $0$ and $1$), the quotient will be **greater** than the dividend! (Because you are asking "How many quarters fit into 6 wholes?", and the answer is a lot!).
