## Applying Fraction Rules

Fractional operations are incredibly useful in everyday life, from cooking to construction. Let's look at how to apply multiplication and division.

### Example 1: Portioning Liquids
Leena made 5 cups of tea. She used $\frac{1}{4}$ litre of milk in total for this. How much milk is in *each* cup?
*   Total milk = $\frac{1}{4}$ L
*   Number of cups = 5
*   Operation: $\frac{1}{4} \div 5$
*   Calculation: $\frac{1}{4} \times \frac{1}{5} = \mathbf{\frac{1}{20}}$ Litre per cup.

### Example 2: Ancient Geometry (Śhulbasūtra)
Cover an area of $7 \frac{1}{2}$ square units with square bricks, where each brick has sides of $\frac{1}{5}$ units. How many bricks are needed?
1. **Area of one brick:** $\frac{1}{5} \times \frac{1}{5} = \frac{1}{25}$ sq units.
2. **Total Area:** $7 \frac{1}{2} = \frac{15}{2}$ sq units.
3. **Number of bricks:** $\text{Total Area} \div \text{Area of one brick}$
   $$ \frac{15}{2} \div \frac{1}{25} = \frac{15}{2} \times \frac{25}{1} = \frac{375}{2} = \mathbf{187.5 \text{ bricks}} $$

## A Pinch of History

Fractions are an essential part of mathematics, and the modern way we calculate with them was developed extensively in ancient India. 

*   **The Śhulbasūtras (c. 800 BCE):** These ancient geometry texts dealt with constructing complex fire altars for rituals. They used non-unit fractions and division extensively.
*   **Umasvati (c. 150 CE):** A Jain scholar who casually referenced *apavartana*—the process of reducing a fraction to its lowest terms.
*   **Brahmagupta (628 CE):** In his *Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta*, he explicitly wrote out the formulas for multiplying and dividing fractions exactly as we use them today!
*   **Bhāskara II (1150 CE):** In his famous book *Līlāvatī*, he clarified fraction division using the concept of the reciprocal.

The Indian theory of fractions was later transmitted to Arab mathematicians (like al-Hassâr of Morocco, who helped popularize the fraction bar line) and eventually to Europe.

**A Dramma-tic Donation (From Bhāskara's Līlāvatī):**
"A miser gave to a beggar $\frac{1}{5}$ of $\frac{1}{16}$ of $\frac{1}{4}$ of $\frac{1}{2}$ of $\frac{2}{3}$ of $\frac{3}{4}$ of a dramma."
To find out how much this is, you multiply all the fractions together!
$$ \left( \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{2}{3} \times \frac{3}{4} \times \frac{1}{5} \times \frac{1}{16} \times \frac{1}{4} \right) = \frac{1}{1280} \text{ of a dramma.} $$
Since 1 dramma = 1280 cowrie shells, the miser gave exactly **1 cowrie shell**!
