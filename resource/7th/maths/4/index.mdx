## Introduction

In this chapter, we transition from pure arithmetic to **Algebra**. We will look at a concise way of expressing mathematical relations and patterns using a combination of numbers and letters. 

Using letters (like $x, y, a, b, n$) allows us to write general rules and formulas that apply to *any* number, rather than just one specific situation. These letters are called **variables** or **letter-numbers**. By the end of this chapter, you will be able to construct mathematical machines, figure out complex repeating patterns, and simplify large algebraic equations!

<Callout variant="info">
**What is an Algebraic Expression?**
Mathematical expressions containing letter-numbers (variables), such as $a + 3$ or $2n + 1$, are called **algebraic expressions**.
</Callout>

## Important Formulas and Rules

Here are some standard formulas we derive by using letter-numbers:

### 1. Perimeters of Regular Shapes
If $s$ represents the length of a side:
*   **Square:** Perimeter $= 4 \times s = \mathbf{4s}$
*   **Equilateral Triangle:** Perimeter $= 3 \times s = \mathbf{3s}$
*   **Regular Pentagon:** Perimeter $= 5 \times s = \mathbf{5s}$
*   **Regular Hexagon:** Perimeter $= 6 \times s = \mathbf{6s}$

If $l$ represents length and $b$ represents breadth:
*   **Rectangle:** Perimeter $= l + b + l + b = \mathbf{2l + 2b}$

### 2. Key Algebraic Rules
*   **Omission of Multiplication Sign:** $4 \times n$ is simply written as $\mathbf{4n}$. The number always comes *before* the letter.
*   **Commutative Property:** You can add terms in any order. $x + y = y + x$.
*   **Like Terms:** Terms with the exact same letter-numbers can be added or subtracted. (e.g., $4v + 3v = \mathbf{7v}$).
*   **Unlike Terms:** Terms with different letter-numbers cannot be combined. (e.g., $18c + 11d$ cannot be simplified further).
