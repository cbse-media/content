## The Problem of Ambiguity

Sometimes, when an expression has multiple operations and no context, there can be more than one way to evaluate it. Let's look at an example:

**Expression:** $30 + 5 \times 4$

*   **Purna's Method:** Purna added $30$ and $5$ first to get $35$, and then multiplied by $4$. $35 \times 4 = \mathbf{140}$.
*   **Mallesh's Method:** Mallesh multiplied $5$ and $4$ first to get $20$, and then added $30$. $30 + 20 = \mathbf{50}$.

Who is right? Just looking at $30 + 5 \times 4$, the order is confusing. Just as punctuation marks (like commas) resolve confusion in language, **brackets** resolve confusion in mathematics.

## Using Brackets in Expressions

Brackets `( )` tell us exactly which part of the expression to evaluate first.

If the situation meant we had $30$ loose marbles and $5$ bags containing $4$ marbles each, the correct notation uses brackets around the multiplication:
$$ 30 + (5 \times 4) $$

<Callout variant="warning">
**Rule of Brackets:**
When evaluating an expression having brackets, we must **first** find the value of the expression *inside* the brackets before performing other operations outside of it.
</Callout>

### Example: Calculating Change
Irfan bought a pack of biscuits for ₹15 and a packet of *toor dal* for ₹56. He gave the shopkeeper a ₹100 note. Write an expression for his change.

*   Total cost = $15 + 56$
*   Money given = $100$

If we write: $100 - 15 + 56$
Evaluating left-to-right: $100 - 15 = 85$. Then $85 + 56 = 141$. He gets ₹141 back? That's absurd; he only paid ₹100!

**Correct Expression with Brackets:**
We must subtract the *total sum* of his items from $100$.
$$ 100 - (15 + 56) $$
Evaluate inside the bracket first: $15 + 56 = 71$.
$$ 100 - 71 = 29 $$
Irfan will get back ₹29.
