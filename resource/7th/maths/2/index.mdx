## Introduction

Mathematical phrases like $13 + 2$, $20 - 4$, $12 \times 5$, and $18 \div 3$ are called **arithmetic expressions**. 

Every arithmetic expression has a **value**, which is the single number it evaluates to. For example, the value of the expression $13 + 2$ is $15$. This expression can be read as ‘13 plus 2’ or ‘the sum of 13 and 2’.

In this chapter, we explore how to construct these expressions, understand the order of operations using terms and brackets, and discover mathematical properties that allow us to calculate large expressions quickly and efficiently.

<Callout variant="tip">
**Equality Sign:**
We use the equality sign `$=$` to denote the relationship between an arithmetic expression and its evaluated value. For example: $13 + 2 = 15$.
</Callout>

## Important Properties and Rules

Memorize these fundamental rules for working with arithmetic expressions:

### 1. Commutative Property of Addition
Swapping the order of terms being added does not change the total sum.
$$ Term_1 + Term_2 = Term_2 + Term_1 $$

### 2. Associative Property of Addition
Grouping the terms of an expression in different ways gives the same value.
$$ (Term_1 + Term_2) + Term_3 = Term_1 + (Term_2 + Term_3) $$

### 3. Removing Brackets
*   **Preceded by a Positive Sign (`+`):** The signs of the terms inside the brackets **do not** change.
    $$ 28 + (35 - 10) = 28 + 35 - 10 $$
*   **Preceded by a Negative Sign (`-`):** The signs of the terms inside the brackets **change**!
    $$ 100 - (15 + 56) = 100 - 15 - 56 $$

### 4. Distributive Property
The multiple of a sum (or difference) is the same as the sum (or difference) of the multiples.
$$ A \times (B + C) = (A \times B) + (A \times C) $$
