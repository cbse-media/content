## The Gap After Harappa

The Indus/Harappan civilisation was India's 'First Urbanisation' (around 2000 BCE). When it declined, people reverted to a rural lifestyle. For a whole millennium, the components of urban life—crowded streets, busy markets, writing systems, and large state administrations—were largely absent from India.

## The Second Urbanisation

In the 1st millennium BCE, a new phase of urbanisation began. It started in the fertile **Ganga plains** and the Indus basin, gradually spreading to other parts of the subcontinent. We know this from two main sources:
1.  **Archaeological excavations** of ancient urban centers.
2.  **Ancient literature** (Late Vedic, Buddhist, and Jain texts).

## Rise of the Janapadas

Towards the end of the 2nd millennium BCE, people formed clans sharing a common language and customs. Each clan settled in a specific territory called a **janapada**, led by a *rājā* (ruler).

As trade networks expanded, these *janapadas* grew. By the 8th or 7th centuries BCE, some states merged to form larger, more powerful units known as **mahājanapadas**.

### The 16 Mahājanapadas

Ancient texts frequently list exactly 16 *mahājanapadas*. They stretched from Gandhāra in the northwest to Anga in the east, and down to Aśhmaka in central India. 

<Callout variant="tip">
**Why the Ganga Plains?**
Most *mahājanapadas* were concentrated in the Ganga plains because:
1. The soil was incredibly fertile for agriculture.
2. Rivers provided excellent transportation and trade routes.
3. Nearby mountains and hills provided vital iron ore for tools and weapons.
</Callout>

These new capitals were often heavily fortified. They featured massive rampart walls, narrow controlled gateways, and deep water-filled **moats** for defense. Amazingly, many of these 2,500-year-old capitals continue to be living cities today!
