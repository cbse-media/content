## The Power of the Assembly

In the early *janapadas*, the ruler (*rājā*) did not have absolute power. Each state had an assembly or council—called a **sabhā** or **samiti**—where important clan matters were discussed. The *rājā* was expected to take advice from these assemblies, which were likely made up of clan elders. 

According to some ancient texts, an incompetent ruler could even be removed by the assembly!

## Two Types of Government

As the *janapadas* expanded into massive *mahājanapadas*, their political systems evolved in two distinct directions:

### 1. Monarchies
In states like **Magadha**, **Kosala**, and **Avanti** (which became the most powerful), the system was a hereditary monarchy. 
*   The *rājā* was the ultimate authority.
*   The position was usually passed down from father to son.
*   The king collected taxes, maintained large armies, and built impressive fortifications around the capital.

### 2. Early Republics (Gaṇas or Sanghas)
In contrast, states like **Vajji (Vṛijji)** and **Malla** had a very different system.
*   They were not ruled by a single hereditary king.
*   The *sabhā* or *samiti* held significant power and made important decisions through discussion and voting.
*   The assembly actually **selected the ruler**!
*   Because members of the assembly made the major decisions, these states functioned somewhat democratically. Scholars often refer to them as **'early republics'**, making them some of the earliest democratic systems in the world.
