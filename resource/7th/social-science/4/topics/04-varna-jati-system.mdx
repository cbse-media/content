## Organizing a Complex Society

As civilizations grow, societies become complex and naturally organize into different groups based on administration, religion, trade, farming, and crafts. In ancient India, this organization evolved into a two-fold system: the **Jāti** and the **Varṇa**.

## 1. The Jāti System
A **jāti** was a community of people whose identity was closely tied to their specific professional occupation (e.g., agriculture, metallurgy, commerce).
*   The skills required for the profession were generally passed down from generation to generation.
*   Over time, *jātis* subdivided into *sub-jātis*, developing their own unique customs, traditions, marriage rules, and food habits.

## 2. The Varṇa System
The **varṇa** concept emerged from ancient Vedic texts. It divided society into four broad categories based on expected duties:
1.  **Brahmins:** Engaged in preserving knowledge, teaching, and performing rituals.
2.  **Kshatriyas:** Expected to defend the society, rule, and engage in warfare.
3.  **Vaishyas:** Tasked with increasing society's wealth through trade, business, and agriculture.
4.  **Shudras:** Artisans, craftspeople, workers, and servants.

### Flexibility vs. Rigidity
Historical evidence shows that in the early period, this system was relatively flexible. 
*   Individuals or entire communities could, and did, change their professions if circumstances demanded it (e.g., due to droughts, farmers might move to cities for other work, or Brahmins might take up military activities).
*   This system initially provided structure and economic stability to Indian society.

<Callout variant="warning">
**The Evolution into 'Caste'**
Over time, the *varṇa-jāti* system lost its flexibility. It became rigid, leading to severe inequalities and discrimination against lower *jātis* and excluded communities. The Portuguese travelers in the 16th century used the word **casta** (caste) to describe this complex, rigid social structure they observed.
