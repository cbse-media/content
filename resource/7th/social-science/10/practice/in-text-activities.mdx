## 1. The Kabaddi Match Rulebook (Page 211)

**LET'S EXPLORE:** *Discuss what might have happened if there was no official rulebook that the referee and the team captains could refer to.*

**Discussion:**
If there were no rulebook, the dispute over whether the player was "out" would rely purely on arguments. The stronger or louder team might force their way, leading to an unfair match, physical fights, or the cancellation of the game. A rulebook provides a **neutral, agreed-upon standard** that everyone respects, ensuring fairness and peace. 

Similarly, for a country, if there were no Constitution, powerful people could make up rules as they pleased, leading to injustice and tyranny. The Constitution ensures everyone plays by the same rules.

## 2. Women's Equality in India (Page 221)

**LET'S EXPLORE:** *Read the quotation by Begum Aizaz Rasul. Which article of the Constitution do you think she was referring to? Why do you think she said that the equality of women was not a new concept for India?*

**Discussion:**
*   **Which Article?** She was primarily referring to **Article 14** (Right to Equality before the law) and **Article 15** (Prohibition of discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth).
*   **Not a new concept?** She stated this because ancient Indian civilisational heritage originally featured great respect and equality for women (with female scholars and thinkers in ancient times). However, over centuries, "social conditions... tragically debased it in practice." The Constitution was a way to legally restore that ancient ideal of equality.

## 3. The National Flag in 2004 (Page 223)

**LET'S EXPLORE:** *There was a time when people were not allowed to fly the national flag at their homes. This changed in 2004 when a citizen challenged the rule in court...*

**Discussion:** 
This example perfectly illustrates two things:
1.  **Fundamental Rights in Action:** The Supreme Court protected the citizen's right under the Fundamental Right to Freedom of Expression (Article 19).
2.  **A Living Document:** It shows how the interpretation of the Constitution evolves. A rule that existed for decades was changed to better reflect the democratic freedoms of the citizens, proving the Constitution is alive and adaptable.

## 4. Applying the Preamble to Daily Life (Page 226)

**LET'S EXPLORE:** *Below is the list of features given in the Preamble. Write down examples of the application of these values in daily life around you.*

**Suggested Answers:**

| Features of the Preamble | How we see them in our daily lives |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Sovereign** | The Indian government decides its own foreign policy, like who to trade with, without taking orders from the USA or UK. |
| **Secular** | People in your neighbourhood celebrate Diwali, Eid, and Christmas openly, and the government declares national holidays for festivals of all religions. |
| **Republic** | We elect a new President every 5 years; the position does not automatically go to the previous President's child. |
| **Justice** | The State provides equal opportunity to all in jobs, regardless of gender, caste, religion, etc. Courts provide a place for poor people to sue rich corporations if wronged. |
| **Liberty** | You have the right to write a blog, publish a newspaper, or peacefully protest against a new law you disagree with. |
| **Equality** | In a government school, children from wealthy families and poor families sit in the same classroom, wear the same uniform, and eat the same mid-day meal. |
| **Fraternity** | During natural disasters like floods, Indians from all over the country send donations and help to the affected state, treating them as fellow brothers and sisters. |
