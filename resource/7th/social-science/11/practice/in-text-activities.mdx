## 1. The Farmer's Difficulties (Page 231)

**LET'S EXPLORE & THINK ABOUT IT:** *Imagine you are a farmer with only an ox to spare. You need shoes, a sweater, and medicines. What difficulties are you likely to face? What are the instances of double coincidence of wants?*

**Discussion:**
*   **Difficulties faced:** The farmer will face the **problem of divisibility** (he cannot cut off a piece of the ox to buy just a sweater), the **problem of portability** (if he trades the ox for 10 sacks of wheat, carrying the wheat to three different shops is exhausting), and the **lack of a common measure of value** (how much ox equals a bottle of medicine?).
*   **Double coincidence of wants:** He must find a shoemaker who specifically needs an ox, a sweater-weaver who specifically needs an ox, etc. If the pharmacist wants cotton, the farmer's ox is useless to him.

## 2. Roman Coins in Tamil Nadu (Page 239)

**LET'S EXPLORE:** *The coins shown in Fig. 11.12 were found during excavations in Pudukkottai in Tamil Nadu. Their heads embossed are those of Roman kings. What conclusions can we draw from such a finding?*

**Discussion:**
Finding Roman coins in deep southern India leads historians to conclude two main things:
1.  **Global Trade:** Ancient Indian kingdoms had extensive, direct maritime trade routes connecting them to the vast Roman Empire.
2.  **Trade Surplus:** Because Roman gold coins were physically left behind and buried in India, it indicates that Romans bought more goods from India (like spices, textiles, and gems) than India bought from Rome. The trade was heavily in India's favour.

## 3. Paper Money Problems (Page 241)

**LET'S EXPLORE:** *What do you think happened as coins began to be used for all types of exchanges, whether to buy vegetables or to buy some land? What problems could have come up?*

**Discussion:**
While coins solved the durability and divisibility problems of barter, they eventually created a **portability problem**. Buying a tiny item like a vegetable was easy with one coin. However, buying a massive asset like a farm or a ship required thousands of gold or copper coins. This made transportation extremely heavy, difficult, and highly vulnerable to highway robbers. This specific problem led directly to the invention of lightweight **paper money**.

## 4. Exploring the ₹50 and ₹100 Notes (Page 242)

**LET'S EXPLORE:** *Look at a 50 and a 100-rupee note. Can you identify the motifs depicting India’s cultural heritage on the reverse side? Feel the surface of the notes. What special features help visually impaired persons?*

**Discussion:**
*   **Cultural Motifs (Reverse Side):**
    *   **₹50 Note:** Features the **Hampi with Chariot**, showcasing the architectural marvel of the ancient Vijayanagara Empire in Karnataka.
    *   **₹100 Note:** Features **Rani Ki Vav** (The Queen's Stepwell), a stunning ancient stepwell located in Patan, Gujarat, recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage site.
*   **Features for the Visually Impaired:**
    The RBI prints notes with **Intaglio (raised) printing**. If you rub your finger over the Mahatma Gandhi portrait or the Ashoka Pillar emblem, you can feel the ink raised above the paper. Additionally, on the edges of the ₹100 note, there are **bleed lines** (four raised angular lines) that visually impaired people can count with their fingernails to identify the denomination.
