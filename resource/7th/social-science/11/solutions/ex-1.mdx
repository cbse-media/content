## Questions and Activities

**1. How does the barter system take place and what kinds of commodities were used for exchange under the system?**

**Answer:** 
The barter system takes place through the direct exchange of goods and services without the use of money. A person trades something they have for something they need from another person. 
Historically, various commodities were used for exchange, including:
*   Agricultural and natural products: cowrie shells, salt, tea, tobacco, and seeds.
*   Livestock: cattle (cows, goats, horses, sheep).
*   Special items: Rai stones (Micronesia), Tajadero copper knives (Aztecs), and Tevau red feather coils (Solomon Islands).

***

**2. What were the limitations of the barter system?**

**Answer:** 
The primary limitations of the barter system are:
1.  **Double coincidence of wants:** Both parties must exactly desire what the other has to offer.
2.  **Lack of a common standard measure of value:** It is difficult to compare the value of two vastly different items (e.g., how much wheat equals one cow).
3.  **Problem of divisibility:** Many goods, like live animals, cannot be divided into smaller pieces without losing their value.
4.  **Problem of portability:** Carrying large quantities of goods (like sacks of grains) for trade is physically difficult.
5.  **Problem of durability:** Goods like vegetables, fruits, or wheat can rot or be destroyed over time, making it impossible to store "wealth."

***

**3. What were the salient features of ancient Indian coins?**

**Answer:** 
*   **Names:** They were known as *kārṣhāpaṇas* or *paṇas*.
*   **Material:** They were minted from precious metals like gold, silver, copper, and their alloys.
*   **Symbols:** They had symbols punched into them, known as *rūpas*.
*   **Design:** They had distinct obverse (head) and reverse (tail) sides featuring motifs of nature (animals, trees), deities, or rulers. For example, Chalukya coins featured the Varaha avatar of Vishnu, while Chola coins featured a tiger emblem.

***

**4. How has money as a medium of exchange transformed over time?**

**Answer:** 
Money has transformed through several stages:
1.  **Commodity Money:** Natural items like shells, salt, and feathers.
2.  **Metal Coinage:** Coins minted from gold, silver, and copper alloys by kings.
3.  **Paper Money:** Lightweight paper currency issued by banks (and later Central Banks like the RBI) to solve the portability issues of heavy coins.
4.  **Digital Money:** Intangible, electronic money transferred via Debit/Credit cards, Net Banking, and UPI using QR codes.

***

**5. What steps might have been taken in ancient times so that Indian coins could become the medium of exchange across countries?**

**Answer:** 
For Indian coins to be accepted internationally, several steps were likely taken:
*   **Standardization:** Maintaining a strict, standard weight and high purity of precious metals (gold/silver) so foreign traders could trust their value.
*   **Maritime Trade:** Indian rulers actively supported and protected maritime trade routes, bringing foreign merchants to Indian ports.
*   **Powerful Kingdoms:** Strong empires (like the Cholas) projected stability, making their currency trustworthy across borders.

**6. Read the following lines from the Arthaśhāstra. “An annual salary of 60 paṇas could be substituted by an āḍhaka of grain per day, enough for four meals…” (One āḍhaka is equal to about 3 kg). What does this indicate about the value of one paṇa? The fine for failing to help a neighbour was 100 paṇas. Compare this with the annual salary. What conclusion can you draw about the human values being encouraged through this?**

**Answer:** 
**Part 1: Value of one paṇa:**
If 1 āḍhaka of grain is given per day, then for a year (approx. 365 days), a worker receives 365 āḍhakas. 
The text states this yearly amount equals 60 paṇas.
$$
\text{Value of 1 paṇa} = \frac{365 \text{ āḍhakas}}{60 \text{ paṇas}} \approx 6 \text{ āḍhakas}
$$
Since 1 āḍhaka = 3 kg, $6 \times 3 = 18$ kg. 
Therefore, **1 paṇa was highly valuable**, equivalent to roughly 18 kg of grain (enough to feed a person for almost a week).

**Part 2: Human Values Conclusion:**
The fine for not helping a neighbour is 100 paṇas, which is **significantly higher than a person's entire annual salary** (60 paṇas). 
*Conclusion:* This massive financial penalty indicates that ancient Indian society placed immense importance on community, fraternity, and mutual assistance. Ignoring a neighbour in need was considered a very severe crime.

**7. Write and enact a skit to show how people may have persuaded each other to use cowrie shells (or other such items) as the medium of exchange.**

**Answer:** 
*(Skit Outline)*
*   **Characters:** Farmer (carrying heavy wheat), Weaver (carrying blankets), Merchant (holding a string of cowrie shells).
*   **Scene:** The Farmer wants a blanket but the Weaver doesn't want wheat. The Weaver wants a clay pot. Both are frustrated.
*   **Action:** The Merchant steps in and shows them lightweight cowrie shells. He explains they don't rot like wheat, they are light to carry, and everyone in the next village accepts them. 
*   **Resolution:** The Farmer trades his wheat to the Merchant for shells. He then gives the shells to the Weaver for the blanket. The Weaver happily takes the shells to the potter. Everyone realizes trading is now much easier!

**8. The RBI is the only legal source that prints and distributes paper currency in India. Find out what some of these security features are and discuss them in class.**

**Answer:** 
To prevent counterfeiting (fake notes), the RBI uses several advanced security features, including:
1.  **Watermark:** A faint image of Mahatma Gandhi visible when the note is held up to the light.
2.  **Security Thread:** A metallic thread woven into the paper that reads 'Bharat' (in Hindi) and 'RBI'. It changes colour from green to blue when tilted.
3.  **Latent Image:** The denominational value (e.g., 500) is hidden in a vertical band and only visible when held flat at eye level.
4.  **Intaglio (Raised) Printing:** The portrait of Gandhi and the Ashoka Pillar emblem are printed with raised ink, which can be felt by touch.
5.  **Micro-lettering:** Tiny letters between the portrait and the vertical band visible only under a magnifying glass.

**9. Interview a few of your family members and local shopkeepers, and ask them their preferences in making and receiving payments—do they prefer cash or UPI? Why?**

**Answer:** 
*(Sample Student Response)*
*   **Shopkeeper's preference:** The local vegetable vendor prefers **UPI** because he doesn't have to worry about finding exact change (coins) for every customer, and there is no risk of receiving a torn or fake currency note.
*   **Grandparent's preference:** My grandfather prefers **Cash** because he is not very comfortable using smartphones and feels that holding physical paper money is safer and easier to track.
*   **Parent's preference:** My mother prefers **UPI** because it is portable—she only needs her phone—and it keeps a digital record of all her spending in her bank app.
