## The Era of Coinage

As people moved away from non-metallic commodity money (like shells and stones), they began using metals. 

### Minting and Alloys
Rulers of different kingdoms took control of producing money by **minting** coins. To make coins strong and durable, they used **alloys** (a metal made by combining two or more metallic elements, like iron, chromium, and carbon). Early coins were often made from precious metals like gold, silver, and copper.

<Callout variant="info">
**Minting:** The process of producing coins. A mint is a manufacturing facility that produces coins used as a nation's currency.
</Callout>

### Ancient Indian Coins
*   Ancient coins were called **kārṣhāpaṇas** or **paṇas**. Interestingly, variations of the word *paṇa* are still used today (*paṇam* in Tamil/Malayalam, *haṇa* in Kannada).
*   They had symbols punched on them called **rūpas**.
*   Coins had an **obverse** (the "head" side featuring the principal design) and a **reverse** (the "tail" side).
*   **Chalukya Coins:** Featured a Varaha image (avatar of Vishnu) on one side and a parasol on the other.
*   **Chola Coins:** Often featured their royal emblem, the tiger.

### Trade and Roman Coins
Excavations in places like Pudukkottai (Tamil Nadu) and Kerala have uncovered gold coins bearing the faces of Roman kings. This proves that ancient India had massive maritime trade with the Roman Empire, and because the coins ended up in India, it shows trade was highly in favour of India!

## The Shift to Paper Money

While coins were highly durable, they posed a new problem: **Portability**. If a merchant needed to buy a large piece of land, carrying thousands of heavy gold or copper coins was dangerous and exhausting. 

The search for a lighter alternative led to **paper money**.
*   Paper money was first used in **China**.
*   It was introduced in India in the late 18th century.
*   Early paper notes in India were issued by banks like the *Bank of Bengal* and *Bank of Bombay*.

Today, a country's system of money is called its **currency**. Coins are generally used for smaller **denominations** (₹1, ₹2, ₹5, ₹10, ₹20), while paper currency is used for higher denominations (₹50, ₹100, ₹500).

<Callout variant="tip">
**Did you know?**
The modern ₹ symbol was adopted by the Government of India in 2010. Designed by Udaya Kumar, it blends the Devanagari "Ra" and the Roman "R", with two parallel horizontal stripes representing the national flag and the "equal to" sign!
</Callout>
