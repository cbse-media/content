## The Empire at its Zenith

Chandragupta's grandson, **Aśhoka** (ruled 268–232 BCE), inherited a vast empire and aggressively expanded it further. His empire covered almost the entire subcontinent, extending into modern-day Afghanistan.

## The Turning Point: The Kalinga War

Aśhoka marched on the independent state of **Kalinga** (modern-day Odisha). He won the war, but the battlefield was a scene of horrific slaughter and destruction. 

Deeply traumatized by the suffering he had caused, Aśhoka did something unprecedented in world history: **He publicly renounced violence and war at the height of his power.**

## The Path of Dhamma

Aśhoka embraced the teachings of the Buddha. He decided to rule his empire not through military terror, but through **Dhamma** (the Prakrit word for *Dharma*). 

For Aśhoka, Dhamma meant:
*   Ruling with compassion, fairness, and impartiality.
*   Treating all subjects as his own "children."
*   Respecting all religious sects and schools of thought.
*   Banning cruelty to animals and stopping royal hunting trips.

## The Great Communicator

To spread his message, Aśhoka became ancient India's greatest communicator. He had his royal orders and philosophical thoughts carved into massive stone pillars and rock faces across the empire. These are known as the **Aśhokan Edicts**.

*   **Language & Script:** Most edicts were written in **Prakrit** (the common language of the people, rather than elite Sanskrit) and carved using the **Brahmi script** (the ancestor of almost all modern Indian alphabets).
*   **Public Works:** His edicts proudly advertise that he built hospitals (for both humans and animals!), dug wells, planted shade trees along highways, and built rest houses for travelers.
*   **Emissaries:** He sent ambassadors to Sri Lanka, Southeast Asia, and even Greek kingdoms in the West to spread Buddhist philosophy and his message of peace.

<Callout variant="info">
**The End of the Empire:**
The Mauryan Empire lasted for about 50 years after Aśhoka's death. Without his strong, unifying presence, and facing the immense cost of maintaining such a vast territory without the loot from new conquests, the empire eventually fractured back into smaller, independent kingdoms around 185 BCE.
