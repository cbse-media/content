## 1. Temperature (Thermometers)

We measure temperature using **thermometers**, reading the data in degrees Celsius ($^\circ\text{C}$) or Fahrenheit ($^\circ\text{F}$).

Meteorologists use specific temperature statistics:
*   **Ambient Temperature:** The temperature of the immediate surroundings at any given moment.
*   **Range of Temperature:** The maximum temperature minus the minimum temperature recorded during a 24-hour period.
*   **Mean Daily Temperature:** (Maximum + Minimum) $\div 2$.

## 2. Precipitation (Rain Gauge)

Rainfall is measured using a **rain gauge**. It consists of a funnel that collects falling rain into a cylindrical measuring tube. 
If the scale on the cylinder reads $5 \text{ mm}$, we say the area received $5 \text{ mm}$ of rainfall in that specific time period.

## 3. Atmospheric Pressure (Barometer)

Atmospheric pressure is measured using a **barometer**, and the unit is usually the **millibar (mb)**. 
*   Normal pressure at the sea coast is around $1013 \text{ mb}$.
*   A pressure dropping below $1000 \text{ mb}$ indicates a "depression" or low-pressure system, which often brings storms or cyclones.

<Callout variant="warning">
**High Altitude Pressure:**
As you climb a mountain, the air gets thinner and the atmospheric pressure drops significantly. At Khardung La in Ladakh ($5600\text{m}$ high), the pressure is only about $650 \text{ mb}$! This means there is less oxygen per breath, which is why travelers must pause to let their bodies **acclimatise**.
</Callout>

## 4. Wind (Wind Vane & Anemometer)

Wind is the movement of air from high pressure to low pressure areas. 
*   **Wind Direction:** Measured by a **wind vane** (or weather vane) or a wind sock at airports. 
*   **Wind Speed:** Measured by an **anemometer**. It has metal cups that catch the wind; the faster they spin, the higher the wind speed, usually calculated in km/h.

## 5. Humidity (Hygrometer)

Humidity is the amount of water vapour in the air. It is usually measured as **Relative Humidity (RH)**, represented as a percentage (%). 
*   $0\%$ RH means absolutely dry air (impossible in nature).
*   $100\%$ RH means the air is completely saturated with water vapour.
*   Humidity is measured using a **hygrometer**.

*Note: High humidity makes it harder for sweat (or wet clothes) to evaporate, which is why humid weather feels sticky and uncomfortable.*
