## The Atmosphere

Before we understand weather, we must understand the atmosphere. The atmosphere is the layer of gases (which we call 'air') that surrounds the Earth. 

The layer closest to the surface, extending 6 to 18 km up, is the **troposphere**. This is where we live, breathe, and where almost all weather happens!

## The Five Elements of Weather

When we say it is "hot", "rainy", or "muggy", we are describing specific elements of the weather. There are five main elements that meteorologists track:

1.  **Temperature:** How hot or cold the atmosphere is.
2.  **Precipitation:** Any form of water that falls from the sky. This includes rain, snow, sleet (frozen or partly frozen rain), and hail (small, hard balls of ice).
3.  **Atmospheric Pressure:** The weight of the air above us, felt on the Earth's surface.
4.  **Wind:** The movement of air, specifically measured by its speed and direction.
5.  **Humidity:** The amount of water vapour (water in a gaseous state) present in the air. 

<Callout variant="tip">
**Why measure scientifically?**
If a friend in Chennai says it is "cold", and you live in Kashmir, their definition of cold might feel like a warm spring day to you! To communicate accurately, we need standardized, scientific measurements.
</Callout>

### How Nature Predicts the Weather

Long before modern instruments, humans observed nature's clues to forecast the weather. 
*   **Ants:** Shifting their eggs to higher ground indicates expected heavy rain.
*   **Frogs:** Croaking loudly in certain forests often signals incoming rain.
*   **Pine Cones:** These respond to humidity. They close tightly in humid conditions to protect their seeds (often before rain) and open wide in dry conditions to release them.
