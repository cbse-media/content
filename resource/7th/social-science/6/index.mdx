## Introduction

In the previous chapter, we witnessed the rise and fall of the mighty Maurya Empire. After Emperor Ashoka's passing, the vast empire gradually weakened and fragmented. Around 185 BCE, the assassination of the last Maurya emperor marked the beginning of a new era. 

This period, lasting from the 2nd century BCE to the 3rd century CE, is often called the **"Age of Reorganisation."** It was a time when the subcontinent was reshaped by the emergence of powerful regional kingdoms, invasions from the northwest, thriving international trade, and a brilliant explosion of art and literature. 

<Callout variant="info">
**Did you know?**
Despite political fragmentation and constant battles between kingdoms, this era witnessed an incredible synthesis of cultures. The blending of Indian and Greek styles in the Gāndhāra School of Art is a perfect example of this cultural confluence!
</Callout>

## Timeline Overview

Here is a quick look at the prominent dynasties that shaped this era:

### Dynasties from within the Subcontinent
*   **Śhungas:** Founded by Puṣhyamitra Śhunga (185 BCE) in the north and central regions. Known for reviving Vedic rituals and patronizing Buddhist art at Bharhut.
*   **Chedis:** Rose to power in Kalinga (modern Odisha). Most famous ruler was Khāravela, a patron of Jainism.
*   **Sātavāhanas:** A powerful dynasty in the Deccan (Andhra, Telangana, Maharashtra). Known for active maritime trade and rock-cut architecture.
*   **The Southern Kingdoms:** The Cholas, Cheras, and Pāṇḍyas ruled the deep south, a period celebrated in Sangam literature.

### Dynasties from outside the Subcontinent
*   **Indo-Greeks:** Left behind by Alexander's campaigns; ruled the northwest and issued coins depicting both Greek and Indian deities.
*   **Śhakas (Indo-Scythians):** Followed the Indo-Greeks in the northwest.
*   **Kuṣhāṇas:** Arrived from Central Asia. King Kaṇiṣhka was their most famous ruler, overseeing the crucial Silk Route and fostering the Gāndhāra and Mathurā schools of art.
