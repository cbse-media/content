## The Deep South

While the north was undergoing political restructuring, the southern region of India (from the 3rd century BCE to the 3rd century CE) saw the rise of three powerful "crowned kings": the **Cholas**, the **Cheras**, and the **Pāṇḍyas**. 

These kingdoms remained largely independent even during the peak of the Maurya Empire.

## The Sangam Age and Literature

This era is famously known as the **Sangam Age**. The word *sangam* translates to 'association' or 'coming together', referring to great assemblies of poets. 
*   **Sangam Literature:** This is the oldest surviving literature in South India. It consists of beautiful anthologies of Tamil poems that describe personal emotions (like love) and societal values (like heroism). 
*   **The Epic Silappadikāram:** The "Tale of the Anklet" is a famous epic from this period. It tells the tragic story of Kaṇṇagi and Kovalan, highlighting principles of justice and the dharma of rulers.

## The Three Kingdoms

### 1. The Cholas
*   **Location:** Ruled the Kaveri delta region.
*   **Key Figure:** King **Karikāla** established supremacy over the other kingdoms.
*   **Achievement:** He built the **Kallaṇai (Grand Anicut)**, an incredible water diversion system on the Kaveri river. This turned the region into the 'rice bowl of the South' and is still in use today!

### 2. The Cheras
*   **Location:** Ruled western Tamil Nadu and Kerala (known as *Keralaputra*).
*   **Economy:** They were heavily involved in international maritime trade, especially exporting spices (pepper), timber, ivory, and pearls to the Roman Empire and West Asia.

### 3. The Pāṇḍyas
*   **Location:** Ruled southern Tamil Nadu with their capital at Madurai.
*   **Significance:** They were a major naval power and were famous for their lucrative **pearl trade**. Greek historians like Megasthenes wrote about their prosperous kingdom and strong administration.

<Callout variant="info">
**Cultural Exchange**
The incredible wealth of these southern kingdoms came from their natural resources and strategic coastal locations. They actively traded with the Roman Empire, exchanging spices and gems for gold and Roman luxury goods!
