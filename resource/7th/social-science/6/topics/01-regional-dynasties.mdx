## The Fall of the Mauryas

After Emperor Ashoka's death, the Maurya Empire began to decline. Around **185 BCE**, the last Maurya emperor was assassinated by his commander-in-chief, **Puṣhyamitra Śhunga**. This event shattered the unified empire, and former tributary kingdoms quickly asserted their independence. The map of India was fundamentally reorganized.

## Surge of the Śhungas

Puṣhyamitra founded the **Śhunga dynasty**, which controlled parts of north and central India. 
*   **Revival of Vedic Rituals:** Puṣhyamitra performed the *aśhvamedha yajña* (horse sacrifice) to declare his supremacy.
*   **Art and Culture:** Despite reviving Vedic practices, the Śhungas patronized Buddhist art. They added beautiful stone railings and carvings to the **Bharhut Stūpa** (in Madhya Pradesh).
*   **Language:** Sanskrit emerged as a preferred language for literature and philosophy during this time (e.g., the *Yoga Sūtras* of Patañjali).

## Coming of the Chedis

In the east, the region of Kalinga (which Ashoka had conquered) regained its power under the **Chedi dynasty**.
*   **King Khāravela:** He was a prominent ruler and a devoted follower of Jainism.
*   **Architecture:** Near Bhubaneswar, the magnificent **Udayagiri-Khandagiri caves** were carved out of solid rock, likely for Jain monks. These caves are masterclasses in 'rock-cut architecture'.
*   **The Hāthīgumphā Inscription:** Carved in Brahmi script, this inscription details Khāravela's military victories and his protection of all religious sects, reflecting a deeply ingrained Indian ethos of tolerance.

## The Sātavāhanas in the Deccan

To the south of the Śhunga empire, the **Sātavāhanas** (sometimes called 'Andhras') established a vast and powerful kingdom across the Deccan plateau (present-day Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Maharashtra).

*   **Matrilineal Naming:** Sātavāhana kings often included their mother's name in their titles, such as the famous king **Gautamīputra Sātakarṇi** (son of Queen Gautamī).
*   **Economic Prosperity:** They controlled the fertile Krishna-Godavari river system. They were a major naval power, conducting extensive maritime trade with the Roman Empire and West Asia. Their coins even featured images of seafaring ships!
*   **Religious Patronage:** While devout followers of Vāsudeva (Kṛiṣhṇa), they granted tax-free land to Buddhist monks, funding the creation of spectacular rock-cut caves like those at **Karla**.

<Callout variant="tip">
**The Origins of Modern Numerals**
Inscriptions found in the Naneghat caves (near Pune) from the Sātavāhana period include early Brahmi numerals. These symbols are some of the earliest evidence showing the shapes that eventually evolved into the modern numerals we use today!
