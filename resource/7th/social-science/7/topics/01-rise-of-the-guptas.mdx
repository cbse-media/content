## A New Power Emerges

By the 3rd century CE, the mighty Kuṣhāṇa Empire in the northwest had weakened. In the region near present-day Uttar Pradesh and Bihar, the **Gupta dynasty** emerged as regional rulers. Over time, they rose to prominence and established a massive empire, making **Pāṭaliputra** (modern Patna) their capital.

## Strategies of Expansion

The Gupta rulers did not just rely on warfare; they used a combination of strategies to build their empire:

### 1. Military Conquest
**Samudragupta**, the father of Chandragupta II, was an ambitious warrior king. His court poet, Harisena, wrote the *Prayāga praśhasti* (a pillar inscription in Allahabad) praising his goal to "unify the Earth". He fought many wars, defeating kings across northern and eastern India. Interestingly, while he absorbed some kingdoms entirely, he reinstated many defeated kings, allowing them to rule as vassals provided they paid him tribute.

### 2. Matrimonial Alliances
Marriage was a powerful political tool. **Chandragupta I** significantly boosted the empire's power through strategic marriages. Later, his granddaughter **Prabhāvatī Gupta** (daughter of Chandragupta II) was married into the neighboring Vākāṭaka kingdom. When her husband died, she became the regent ruler, ensuring a strong, peaceful alliance between the Guptas and the Vākāṭakas on their southern border.

### 3. Grand Titles and Rituals
To assert their supreme authority over other regional kings, Gupta emperors adopted grand titles like *mahārājadhirāja* (king of kings) and *chakravartin*. They also performed the ancient Vedic *aśhvamedha yajña* (horse sacrifice) to declare their absolute supremacy, commemorating the event by minting special gold coins featuring the sacrificial horse.

**What is a Regent Ruler?**
A regent temporarily governs a kingdom for a monarch who is unable to do so (for example, if the true heir is still a young child). Prabhāvatī Gupta successfully ruled as a regent for her two sons!
