## Beyond the Gupta Borders

While the Gupta Empire dominated northern and central India, several other powerful and culturally vibrant kingdoms thrived in the rest of the subcontinent.

### 1. The Vākāṭakas (Central India)
Located immediately south of the Gupta Empire (in modern Maharashtra/Nagpur), the Vākāṭakas were powerful allies. As we learned, Chandragupta II's daughter, **Prabhāvatī Gupta**, became their regent ruler. 
The Vākāṭakas were great builders, associated with the construction of the famous Kevala Narasimha temple at Ramagiri and providing significant support for the carving of the Ajanta Caves.

### 2. The Pallavas (The South)
As the older Sātavāhana dynasty declined in the south, the **Pallavas** emerged, ruling over parts of Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, and Andhra Pradesh.
*   **Capital:** **Kānchipuram**, known as the 'city of a thousand temples'.
*   **Culture:** They established *ghaṭikās* (centers of learning) and were great patrons of Śhiva, constructing magnificent rock-cut caves and temples that laid the foundation for classical South Indian architecture.

### 3. The Kāmarūpa Kingdom (The Northeast)
In the northeast, the **Varman dynasty** ruled the Kāmarūpa kingdom, covering the Brahmaputra valley in modern-day Assam.
*   The region was an ancient, prominent cultural center mentioned in epics like the *Rāmāyaṇa* and *Mahābhārata* (under the name Prāgjyotiṣha).
*   During Samudragupta's military campaigns, he defeated the ruler of Kāmarūpa but chose not to annex the territory, allowing them to remain independent as long as they paid tribute.

<Callout variant="warning">
**The Decline of the Guptas**
By the 6th century CE, the "Golden Age" began to fade. The empire faced repeated, devastating invasions from the fierce **Hūṇa** tribe entering from Central Asia. These attacks weakened the central authority, leading regional rulers to declare independence, and causing the vast Gupta Empire to slowly disintegrate.
