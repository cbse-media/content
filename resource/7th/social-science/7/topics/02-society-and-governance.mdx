## Governance and Administration

The Gupta Empire was vast, and managing it required a well-organized system. Unlike earlier empires that tried to control everything centrally, the Guptas **decentralized** their administration.
*   They divided the empire into provinces.
*   They granted land and administrative power to local rulers, priests, and chieftains. 
*   These land grants were carefully recorded on **copper plates**, serving as official legal documents.

This system ensured efficient tax collection and local control while maintaining the ultimate authority of the Emperor. 

### Thriving Trade
The primary revenue for the empire was **land tax**, but trade was also a massive source of wealth. The Guptas maintained vibrant internal trade networks and exported textiles, spices, ivory, and gemstones to the Mediterranean world, Southeast Asia, and China via overland routes and the Indian Ocean.

## A Traveller’s Account: Faxian

Much of what we know about daily life in the Gupta period comes from the travelogue of **Faxian** (Fa-Shi-Anne), a Chinese Buddhist pilgrim who visited India in the early 5th century CE.

According to his records of the Gangetic plains:
*   The people were "numerous and happy," and the government was not overly oppressive.
*   The cities were prosperous, and the wealthy merchant classes (Vaishyas) established charity houses and free hospitals for the poor and orphaned.
*   The overall society was peaceful and strictly managed.

<Callout variant="warning">
**The Reality of Outcastes**
While Faxian praised the prosperity of the cities, he also documented the dark side of the social structure. He described the harsh treatment of the *chanḍālas*, who were regarded as **outcastes**—people considered socially too low to even be part of the four-tier *varṇa* system. They were forced to live outside the city limits and were heavily discriminated against.
