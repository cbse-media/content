## Questions and Activities

**1. What are the main features of a market? Recall a recent visit to a market to purchase a product. What are the different features of a market that you observed during this visit?**

**Answer:**
The main features of a market are:
1.  Presence of **Buyers** (who demand goods).
2.  Presence of **Sellers** (who supply goods).
3.  An agreed-upon **Price** for the transaction.
4.  Exchange of goods or services for money.

*Recall Example:* During a recent visit to a local vegetable market, I observed sellers shouting out their prices to attract buyers. I saw buyers examining the quality of tomatoes and negotiating (bargaining) the price with the seller before paying in cash or via a UPI QR code.

**2. Remember the epigraph from a famous economist at the beginning of the chapter? Discuss its relevance in the context of the chapter you have read.**

**Answer:**
The epigraph is: *"Prosperity emanates from the market that develops when people need goods and services that they can’t create themselves."* - Adam Smith.
**Relevance:** This chapter shows that no individual can produce everything they need (food, clothes, electronics). Because of this need, markets develop. As seen in the example of the Hampi Bazaar or the Surat Textile Market, when people gather to buy and sell specialized goods, it creates jobs, spreads wealth, and brings prosperity and development to the entire society.

**3. In the example of buying and selling of guavas, imagine that the seller is getting a good price, and is able to make a profit. He will try to get more guavas from farmers to be able to sell them at the same price and increase his earnings. What is the farmer likely to do in this kind of a situation? Do you think he will start thinking about the demand for guavas in the next season? What is likely to be his response?**

**Answer:**
If the seller is buying more guavas at a profitable price, the farmer will realize that the **demand** for guavas is high. 
*   **Response:** In the next season, the farmer is highly likely to allocate more land and resources to grow a larger quantity of guavas. He will increase his **supply** to meet the high market demand, hoping to maximize his own profits.

**5. Prices are generally determined by the interaction between demand from buyers and supply by sellers. Can you think of products where prices are high despite lesser number of buyers demanding the product? What could be the reasons for that?**

**Answer:**
Yes, products like diamond jewelry, luxury sports cars, original oil paintings, and rare antique coins have very high prices despite having very few buyers.
**Reasons:**
1.  **Extreme Scarcity (Low Supply):** The raw materials (like flawless diamonds) are extremely rare and difficult to extract. 
2.  **High Cost of Production:** It takes immense time, highly skilled labor, and expensive technology to create these items.
3.  **Exclusivity:** The few buyers who *do* want them are willing to pay massive amounts of money just to own something unique.

**6. Look at the real life situation that a retail seller of vegetables encountered... The family then goes to a super bazaar nearby where they pay ₹40/kg for beans packed in a plastic bag. What are the reasons that the family does this? Are there factors that affect buying and selling which are not directly connected to price?**

**Answer:**
The family refused to pay ₹30/kg at the cart but happily paid ₹40/kg at the super bazaar. 
**Reasons & Factors:**
*   **Perception of Quality & Hygiene:** The beans in the super bazaar were neatly packed, making them appear cleaner, safer, and of better quality.
*   **Shopping Experience:** The super bazaar offers a comfortable, air-conditioned environment, shopping carts, and the convenience of buying many different items under one roof.
*   **Fixed Pricing:** People are often less inclined to bargain in a formal store with printed price tags, whereas bargaining with a street vendor is a cultural habit.
Yes, factors like convenience, packaging, store ambiance, hygiene, and brand trust heavily affect buying decisions, even if the price is higher.

**7. With a large quantity of harvest, there are reports of farmers throwing away their produce (tomatoes)... Why do you think farmers do this? What role can wholesalers play in such situations? What are the possible ways of ensuring that the tomatoes are not wasted, and the farmers are also not at a loss?**

**Answer:**
*   **Why throw them away?** When there is massive over-supply, the price of tomatoes drops so low (e.g., ₹1/kg) that it doesn't even cover the transportation cost to take them to the market. Throwing them away saves transport money. Also, tomatoes rot quickly.
*   **Role of Wholesalers:** Wholesalers with access to **Cold Storage** facilities can buy the excess tomatoes at a fair price and safely store them for months, releasing them into the market slowly when supply drops.
*   **Preventing Waste:** 
    1. The government could set a **minimum support price (price floor)**.
    2. Tomatoes can be sold to processing factories to be converted into non-perishable goods like ketchup, purees, and sauces.
