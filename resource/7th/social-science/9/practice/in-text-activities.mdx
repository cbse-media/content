## 1. The School Example

**THINK ABOUT IT:** *The students' committee is different from Parliamentary and Legislative committees, as class representatives in a school are quite different from Members of Parliament (MPs) and Members of the Legislative Assembly (MLAs). What are these differences?*

**Discussion:**
*   **Scale and Scope:** A school representative handles small-scale issues like timetables or events for a few hundred students. MPs and MLAs represent millions of people and handle national/state issues like defence, economy, and healthcare.
*   **Legal Authority:** Laws passed by MPs/MLAs are legally binding and enforced by police/courts. School committee rules are enforced by teachers and principals.
*   **Age and Qualifications:** MPs and MLAs must meet constitutional age requirements and legal qualifications, whereas school reps are simply current students of that age group.

## 2. Choosing Representatives

**LET'S EXPLORE:** *Which of the three methods to form a student committee do you think is most effective? Why?*

**Discussion:**
The **third method** (Students from each grade choosing one representative through voting) is the most effective. 
*   *Why?* If everyone is on the committee (Method 1), it leads to chaos and nothing gets decided. If the Head Teacher picks them (Method 2), it is unfair and the students' true voices might not be heard. Method 3 is **democratic**, practical, and ensures every grade has fair representation.

## 4. The King with Divine Powers

**THINK ABOUT IT:** *What might happen if the king considered himself to have divine powers? How would he rule over the people?*

**Discussion:**
If a king believes he has divine powers (power given directly by God), he is likely to rule as an **absolute monarch** or a dictator. He would believe that questioning his decisions is equivalent to questioning God. This leads to arbitrary rules, severe punishments for critics, and a lack of freedom or fundamental rights for the common people.

## 5. Story of Shane (North Korea)

**LET'S EXPLORE:** 
1. *Does Shane’s country look like a democracy?* 
   **Answer:** No. Shane's country represents a dictatorship.
2. *How do you think Shane’s everyday life would be?* 
   **Answer:** His life is strictly controlled. He lacks basic freedoms—he cannot choose his career (compulsory military), his clothes, his haircut, or access global information. He lives under constant surveillance and fear.
3. *Would you like to live in such a country? Explain why.* 
   **Answer:** No. Living without the freedom to express oneself, access information, or make personal choices strips away human dignity and personal growth.

## 6. Oligarchy Cartoon

**LET'S EXPLORE:** *Look carefully at the picture in Fig. 9.13. What do you observe? Can a democracy turn into an oligarchy? What can people do to keep democracy strong?*

**Discussion:**
*   **Observation:** The cartoon depicts wealthy "Monopolists" (representing huge trusts like Copper, Steel, Standard Oil) looming over and controlling the much smaller elected Senators. The sign reads "This is a Senate of the Monopolists, by the Monopolists, and for the Monopolists."
*   **Can a democracy turn into an oligarchy?** Yes. If massive corporations or ultra-wealthy individuals use their money to buy political influence, fund campaigns, and write laws, the elected officials end up serving the rich few rather than the voting public.
*   **Keeping Democracy Strong:** Citizens must stay informed, vote for leaders with integrity, support laws that limit money in politics, and demand total transparency and accountability from their government.
