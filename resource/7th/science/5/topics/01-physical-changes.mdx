## What is a Physical Change?

Imagine taking a square piece of paper and folding it into a paper airplane. The shape has changed completely! But if you unfold it, it is still the exact same piece of paper. The material itself hasn't changed.

<Callout variant="tip">
**Definition:**
A change in which only the physical properties of a substance—such as its shape, size, color, or state (solid, liquid, gas)—are altered, and **no new substance is formed**, is called a **physical change**.
</Callout>

### Examples of Physical Changes

1.  **Changing Shape or Size:**
    *   Crushing a piece of chalk into dust. (It is still chalk, just in powder form).
    *   Chopping vegetables.
    *   Inflating and deflating a balloon.
2.  **Changing States of Matter:**
    *   Melting ice cubes into water.
    *   Boiling water to form steam (water vapor).
    *   Condensing steam back into liquid water. 
    *(In all these cases, the chemical formula remains $H_2O$. Only the physical state changes!)*

## Are Physical Changes Reversible?

Many physical changes can be easily reversed to get the original object or substance back. 
*   *Reversible:* You can refreeze melted ice to get an ice cube back.
*   *Irreversible:* If you tear a piece of paper into tiny shreds, it is still a physical change (no new substance was made), but you cannot easily put the paper back together. 

Therefore, while physical changes are *often* reversible, they are not *always* reversible.
