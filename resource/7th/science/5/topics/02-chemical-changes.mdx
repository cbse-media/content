## What is a Chemical Change?

When you bake a cake, the raw batter made of flour, eggs, and sugar transforms in the oven into a spongy, delicious treat. You can never separate the baked cake back into raw eggs and flour! The ingredients have reacted to form something entirely new.

<Callout variant="tip">
**Definition:**
A change in which **one or more new substances are formed** is called a **chemical change**. New substances are formed through a process called a **chemical reaction**.
</Callout>

## Fun Experiments with Chemical Changes

### 1. The Breath Test (Lime Water)
If you take a glass of clear lime water (calcium hydroxide solution) and blow air into it using a straw, the water slowly turns milky or cloudy. 
*   **Why?** The air you exhale contains Carbon Dioxide ($CO_2$). The $CO_2$ reacts with the lime water to form a completely new, white, insoluble substance called **Calcium Carbonate**. 
*   This is the standard laboratory test to confirm the presence of Carbon Dioxide gas!

### 2. The Fizzing Potion (Vinegar and Baking Soda)
Take a teaspoon of vinegar (which contains acetic acid) and add a pinch of baking soda to it. 
*   You will instantly hear a fizzing sound and see bubbles rushing to the top.
*   These bubbles are **Carbon Dioxide gas** being rapidly produced by the chemical reaction.
*   If you pass this gas through a tube into a test tube of lime water, the lime water will turn milky, proving that a chemical change occurred!

## Signs of a Chemical Change
How do you know a chemical change has happened? Look for these clues:
*   Gas bubbles are released.
*   There is a permanent change in color (like a banana getting brown spots).
*   Heat or light is given off.
*   A new smell is produced (like food spoiling).
*   A solid substance (precipitate) forms in a liquid.
