## Classifying Changes

Beyond just "Physical" and "Chemical", scientists classify changes in other helpful ways based on human needs and reversibility.

### 1. Reversible vs. Cannot be Reversed
*   **Reversible Changes:** You can get the original object back. (e.g., Melting ice, boiling water).
*   **Cannot be Reversed:** The original object is permanently altered. (e.g., Making popcorn, chopping wood, cooking an egg).

### 2. Desirable vs. Undesirable
*   **Desirable Changes:** Useful changes that help us. (e.g., Milk turning to curd, fruit ripening, cooking food).
*   **Undesirable Changes:** Changes that cause harm or waste. (e.g., Food decaying, iron rusting, metal corroding). 
*   *Note:* A change can be undesirable in one context but desirable in another! Decaying food is bad in your fridge, but it is highly desirable when creating nutrient-rich compost for a garden.

## Slow Natural Changes

Nature performs physical and chemical changes on a massive scale over thousands of years!

![Detailed diagram of a rocky cliff base showing heaps of sand, soil, and broken stones known as sediments](./placeholder.png)

### Weathering of Rocks
Mountains are slowly broken down into smaller stones, sand, and eventually soil. This is called **weathering**.
*   **Physical Weathering:** Freezing water in rock cracks expands and breaks the rock. Temperature changes and tree roots also physically crack the stones.
*   **Chemical Weathering:** Water and atmospheric chemicals react with the minerals in the rock. For example, black basalt rock containing iron reacts with oxygen and water vapor over centuries to form a red-colored layer of iron oxide!

### Erosion
When rock pebbles, soil, and sediments are broken down, they are moved from one location to another by natural forces like wind and flowing rivers. This movement is called **erosion**.
*   Erosion is primarily a **physical change**. 
*   As river rocks tumble in the water, the constant friction physically grinds them down, making them round and smooth over time.
