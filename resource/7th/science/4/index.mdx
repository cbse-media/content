## Introduction

Look around you. The tools we use for farming, the wires that carry electricity to our homes, the jewelry we wear, and even the bridges we drive across are all made of specific materials chosen for their unique properties. 

In this chapter, we step into the world of **Metals** and **Non-metals**. We will explore what makes an iron nail bend when hit with a hammer, why copper is used for electrical wiring, and why coal shatters into pieces. We will also dive into the chemical reactions that happen when these materials are exposed to air, water, and fire!

<Callout variant="info">
**Did you know?**
Metals and non-metals are sub-categories of substances called **elements**. An element is a pure substance that cannot be broken down into simpler substances. Currently, there are $118$ known elements that act as the building blocks for all matter in the universe!
</Callout>

## Quick Reference: Property Comparison

Here is a quick summary of the differences between Metals and Non-metals:

| Property | Metals | Non-metals |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Appearance** | Shiny (Lustrous) | Dull (Non-lustrous) |
| **Hardness** | Generally hard and solid (except Sodium, Potassium, and Mercury) | Generally soft or brittle |
| **Malleability** | Malleable (can be beaten into thin sheets) | Brittle (break into pieces when hammered) |
| **Ductility** | Ductile (can be drawn into thin wires) | Non-ductile |
| **Sonority** | Sonorous (produce a ringing sound when struck) | Non-sonorous (produce a dull sound) |
| **Conduction of Heat** | Good conductors | Poor conductors |
| **Conduction of Electricity** | Good conductors | Poor conductors |
| **Nature of Oxides** | Basic oxides | Acidic oxides |
