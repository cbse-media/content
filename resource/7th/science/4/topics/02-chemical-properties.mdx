## Reaction with Oxygen

When elements burn in the air, they react with oxygen to form new compounds called **oxides**. However, the nature of the oxides formed by metals is very different from those formed by non-metals!

### Burning a Metal (Magnesium)
When a strip of **magnesium ribbon** is ignited, it burns with a dazzling white flame and leaves behind a white powder.
1.  **Reaction:** Magnesium + Oxygen $\rightarrow$ Magnesium Oxide
2.  **Testing the Oxide:** If you dissolve this white powder (Magnesium Oxide) in a few drops of water and test it with red and blue litmus paper, you will observe that the **red litmus paper turns blue**.
3.  **Conclusion:** The solution is basic. **Generally, oxides of metals are basic in nature.**

### Burning a Non-metal (Sulfur)
When a small amount of powdered **sulfur** is heated in a deflagrating spoon, it catches fire and produces a gas.
1.  **Reaction:** Sulfur + Oxygen $\rightarrow$ Sulfur Dioxide gas.
2.  **Testing the Oxide:** If you capture this gas in a jar, add water, and shake it, the gas dissolves to form sulfurous acid.
3.  **Testing the Solution:** Testing this solution with litmus paper shows that the **blue litmus paper turns red**.
4.  **Conclusion:** The solution is acidic. **Generally, oxides of non-metals are acidic in nature.**

<Callout variant="warning">
**The Sodium Exception:**
Sodium is a highly reactive metal. If exposed to air or moisture, it reacts vigorously, generating a massive amount of heat and catching fire! To prevent this dangerous reaction, sodium is stored completely submerged in **kerosene**.
</Callout>

## Reaction with Water

We know that some metals (like iron) rust when exposed to water and air. But how do non-metals react with water?

Generally, **non-metals do not react with water**. 
In fact, water is often used to protect highly reactive non-metals! For example, **Phosphorus** is a very reactive non-metal that catches fire if exposed to the oxygen in the atmospheric air. To prevent this, phosphorus is safely stored underwater.
