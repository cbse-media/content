## 1. Appearance and Hardness

If you look at pieces of copper, aluminium, and iron, you will notice they have a shiny appearance. This shine is called **metallic lustre**. Materials like coal, sulfur, and wood do not have this shine; they are **non-lustrous**.

Most metals are hard and solid. However, there are fascinating exceptions!
*   **Sodium and Potassium:** These metals are so soft they can be cut with a knife.
*   **Mercury:** This is the only metal that is a liquid at room temperature (commonly used in older thermometers).

## 2. Malleability

What happens if you hit an iron nail with a hammer? It flattens out. But if you hit a piece of coal, it shatters into pieces.

<Callout variant="info">
**Definition:** The property by which materials can be beaten into thin sheets is called **malleability**.
</Callout>

*   **Metals** are malleable. You see this everyday: the thin silver foil decorating sweets, or the aluminium foil used to wrap food. **Gold and silver** are the most malleable metals known.
*   **Non-metals** (like coal or sulfur) are **brittle**. They break into pieces when struck.

## 3. Ductility

Where do you find metal wires? Inside electrical cables, or forming the strings of musical instruments like guitars and sitars.

<Callout variant="info">
**Definition:** The property of materials by which they can be drawn out into thin wires is called **ductility**.
</Callout>

*   **Metals** are ductile. **Gold** is so incredibly ductile that just one gram of it can be drawn into a wire $2 \text{ kilometres}$ long!
*   **Non-metals** are not ductile. You cannot make a wire out of coal!

## 4. Sonority

If you drop a metal coin on the floor, it produces a ringing sound. If you drop a piece of wood, it makes a dull thud.

<Callout variant="info">
**Definition:** The property of metals that enables them to produce a ringing sound when struck is called **sonority**.
</Callout>

*   **Metals** are **sonorous**. This is why school bells and musical *ghungroos* are made of metal.
*   **Non-metals** are non-sonorous.

## 5. Conduction of Heat and Electricity

If you leave a metal spoon in a cup of hot water, the handle quickly becomes hot. This is because heat transfers easily through the metal.

*   **Metals** are **good conductors of heat**. This is why cooking vessels are made of metals like copper or aluminium.
*   **Non-metals** (like wood or plastic) are **poor conductors of heat** (insulators). This is why the handles of cooking pans are made of wood or plastic so you don't burn your hands.

Similarly, metals allow electric current to pass through them easily.
*   **Metals** are **good conductors of electricity**. Silver, copper, and gold are the best conductors. Copper is used for most wiring because it is affordable and abundant.
*   **Non-metals** are **poor conductors of electricity**. Plastic and rubber are used to coat electrical wires to protect us from electric shocks.
