## Project 1: Modeling the Seasons

**The Challenge:** Understand visually how the tilt of the Earth's axis creates different seasons as it revolves around the Sun.

**What you need:**
*   A globe
*   A strong electric lamp (without a lampshade) to act as the Sun.
*   A dark room.

**How to do it:**
1.  Place the lamp in the center of the room.
2.  Hold the globe on one side of the lamp. Ensure the axis (the metal rod passing through the poles) is **tilted** slightly away from vertical. 
    *Crucial step: Keep the globe facing the exact same wall of the room for the entire experiment! Do not twist the base.*
3.  **Position 1 (Winter Solstice for India):** Walk the globe to a position where the Northern Hemisphere (where India is) is tilted *away* from the lamp. 
    *   *Observation:* Notice how the light hits the Southern Hemisphere directly, while India gets weak, glancing light. Spin the globe—notice that India stays in the dark shadow for a longer period of the spin than it does in the light.
4.  **Position 2 (Summer Solstice for India):** Walk the globe halfway around the lamp (to the opposite side of the room). Remember to keep the tilt facing the same wall! Now, the Northern Hemisphere should be tilted *towards* the lamp.
    *   *Observation:* Notice how the light hits India intensely and directly. Spin the globe—notice that India is in the light for much longer than it is in the dark!

## Project 2: The Myth of the Oval Orbit

Many people think winter happens because the Earth gets farther away from the Sun. Let's draw the orbit to scale to see if this makes sense!

**The Data:**
The Earth's orbit is technically an ellipse (oval), but how oval is it really?
*   Closest distance to Sun (Perihelion): ~147 million km.
*   Farthest distance from Sun (Aphelion): ~152 million km.

**The Activity:**
Let's use a scale where $1 \text{ cm} = 10 \text{ million km}$.
1.  Take a compass and a piece of paper.
2.  Draw a circle with a radius of **$14.7 \text{ cm}$**. (This represents the closest the Earth gets).
3.  Keep the compass needle in the exact same center spot. Open the compass to **$15.2 \text{ cm}$** and draw a second circle.

**Observation:**
Look at the two circles. The gap between them is only $0.5 \text{ cm}$! The Earth's orbit is nearly a perfect circle. The difference in distance between summer and winter is so tiny that it has almost zero effect on the temperature of the Earth. The seasons are caused entirely by the **tilt** of the axis, not the distance!

<Callout variant="info">
**Fun Fact (Irony):**
The Earth actually reaches its closest point to the Sun (Perihelion) in early **January**—right when the Northern Hemisphere is experiencing its freezing Winter!
</Callout>
