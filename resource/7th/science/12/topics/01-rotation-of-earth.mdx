## The Illusion of Movement

When you ride a merry-go-round that spins anti-clockwise, the trees and buildings around you appear to be spinning clockwise! The objects aren't moving; *you* are.

This is exactly what happens with the Sun, Moon, and stars. 
When we stand on Earth, the Sun appears to rise in the East, move across the sky, and set in the West. But the Sun isn't moving around the Earth! Instead, **the Earth is spinning (rotating) on its axis from West to East (anti-clockwise).**

<Callout variant="tip">
**Definition:**
**Rotation** is the motion of an object in which all its parts move in circles around an imaginary line that passes through it. This line is called the **axis of rotation**.
</Callout>

*   The Earth completes one full rotation on its axis in exactly **24 hours**.
*   This rotation is what causes the cycle of **Day and Night**.

## How Day and Night Occur

Imagine shining a torch at a globe in a dark room. 
*   The side of the globe facing the torch lights up. This represents **Daytime**.
*   The other half of the globe is in shadow. This represents **Nighttime**.

As the Earth rotates from West to East, different parts of the planet move into the sunlight (Sunrise) and then eventually move into the darkness (Sunset). Because India is in the East, places like Arunachal Pradesh experience sunrise before places in the West like Gujarat!

## The Movement of the Stars

Because the Earth rotates, the stars also appear to move across the sky from East to West. However, there is one star that hardly seems to move at all: the **Pole Star (Dhruva Tara)**.

*Why?* The Earth's axis of rotation points almost exactly at the Pole Star. Imagine spinning an umbrella on its tip; the tip doesn't seem to move, while the edges spin around it. Because the Pole Star aligns with our axis, it appears stationary in the Northern Hemisphere, and all other stars appear to rotate around it!
