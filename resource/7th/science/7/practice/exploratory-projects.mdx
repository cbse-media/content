## Activity 1: The Magic Fireproof Paper

**The Experiment:**
Tightly wrap a thin paper strip around a metallic rod (like a thick iron nail or a metal pipe). Try to burn the paper with a candle while rotating the rod continuously. 

**Does the paper burn? Explain your observations.**

**Observation:**
Surprisingly, the paper will **not** burn (or will take a very long time to even scorch), even though paper is highly combustible!

**The Science (Explanation):**
To burn, the paper must reach its ignition temperature. However, the metal rod inside is an excellent **conductor of heat**. As the candle flame heats the paper, the metal rod immediately conducts that heat away from the paper and spreads it throughout its own mass. Because the metal steals the heat so rapidly, the paper never gets hot enough to reach its ignition temperature and catch fire!

*(Note: If you wrapped the paper around a wooden stick, which is an insulator, the heat would stay on the paper, and it would catch fire instantly!)*

## Activity 2: The Dancing Spiral

**The Experiment:**
1.  Take a sheet of paper and draw a spiral on it.
2.  Cut the paper along the spiral line so it dangles like a spring.
3.  Suspend the paper spiral by a thread above a burning candle (ensure it is high enough not to catch fire!).

**Observe what happens. Provide an explanation for your observation.**

**Observation:**
The paper spiral will begin to spin and twirl continuously on its own, like a dancing snake!

**The Science (Explanation):**
This is a perfect visual demonstration of **Convection**.
The flame of the candle heats the air directly above it. Because hot air expands and becomes less dense (lighter) than the surrounding cold air, it **rises rapidly** upwards. As this column of hot air rises, it pushes against the angled cuts of the paper spiral, causing it to spin like a windmill or a turbine. As long as the candle burns, the convection current will keep the spiral dancing!

**Ice Stupas in Ladakh**
In the cold, dry desert of Ladakh, engineers have used a deep understanding of natural processes to solve water scarcity. They pipe winter stream water downhill and spray it into the freezing air. The water freezes layer by layer, forming a massive ice cone called an **Ice Stupa**. Because of its shape, it minimizes surface area exposed to solar **radiation**, allowing the ice to melt slowly over the dry spring months, providing a steady water supply for farmers!
