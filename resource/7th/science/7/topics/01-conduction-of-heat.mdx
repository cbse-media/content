## What is Conduction?

Imagine heating one end of a long iron strip with a candle. Slowly, the heat travels along the strip until the other end also becomes too hot to touch! 

<Callout variant="tip">
**Definition:** The process of heat transfer from the hotter part of an object to the colder part without the actual movement of the particles from their positions is called **conduction**.
</Callout>

In solids, particles are packed tightly together. When one end is heated, the particles vibrate vigorously and pass this energy (heat) to their neighboring particles, spreading the heat throughout the object.

## Good Conductors vs. Poor Conductors

Not all materials conduct heat equally well!

### Good Conductors
Materials that allow heat to pass through them easily are called **good conductors** of heat.
*   **Examples:** All metals (Iron, Copper, Aluminium, Steel).
*   **Use:** We use metal utensils for cooking so that heat from the stove is quickly transferred to the food.

### Poor Conductors (Insulators)
Materials that do not allow heat to pass through them easily are called **poor conductors** (or insulators) of heat.
*   **Examples:** Wood, glass, plastic, clay, porcelain, and **air**.
*   **Uses:**
    *   Tea or coffee kept in clay or porcelain cups stays hot longer because the heat cannot easily escape through the cup.
    *   Cooking pans have wooden or plastic handles so we can hold them safely.
    *   **Woollen clothing:** Wool traps a lot of air in its pores. Since air is a poor conductor of heat, it prevents our body heat from escaping into the cold surroundings, keeping us warm!

<Callout variant="info">
**Why two thin blankets instead of one thick one?**
Two thin blankets trap a layer of air between them. Because air is an excellent insulator, this trapped air layer provides better insulation against the cold than a single thick blanket of the same total thickness!
</Callout>

### Building Insulation
In regions with extreme climates, houses are sometimes built with outer walls made of **hollow bricks**. The air trapped inside the hollow bricks acts as an insulator, keeping the house cool in the summer and warm in the winter.
