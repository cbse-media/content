## Introduction

Why does a metal spoon get hot when left in a bowl of hot soup? Why do people living near the sea experience a cool breeze during the hot day? And how does the water from oceans reach the mountain tops as snow?

All of these phenomena are connected by one fundamental concept: **Heat Transfer**. Heat naturally flows from hotter objects to colder objects. In this chapter, we will explore the three primary ways heat travels: Conduction, Convection, and Radiation. We will also see how these processes drive massive natural systems like the water cycle and groundwater replenishment!

<Callout variant="info">
**Did you know?**
In the upper regions of the Himalayas, traditional room heaters called *bukhari* are used. They burn wood or charcoal inside an iron stove to keep the room warm. All three processes of heat transfer (conduction through the iron, convection of the hot air, and radiation from the stove) are involved when using this device!
</Callout>

## Quick Reference: The Three Modes of Heat Transfer

| Process | Definition | Medium Required? | Common Examples |
| :--- | :--- | :---: | :--- |
| **Conduction** | Heat transfer from one particle to the next *without* the actual movement of the particles. | Yes (mostly Solids) | A metal spoon getting hot; ironing clothes. |
| **Convection** | Heat transfer by the *actual movement* of hot particles rising and cold particles sinking. | Yes (Liquids & Gases) | Boiling water; hot air balloons; smoke rising. |
| **Radiation** | Heat transfer that travels directly as waves/rays. | **No** (Can travel through vacuum) | Heat from the Sun; warmth felt near a fire. |
