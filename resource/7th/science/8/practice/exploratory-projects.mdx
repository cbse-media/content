## Project 1: Build a Ghatika-Yantra (Floating Water Clock)

**The Challenge:**
Construct a floating bowl-type water clock. Experiment by using bowls of different sizes and making holes of different sizes in them so that the sinking time of the bowl is as close to **24 minutes** as possible.

**How to do it:**
1.  Find a large bucket or basin and fill it with water.
2.  Take a small, lightweight plastic or metal bowl. 
3.  Carefully pierce a tiny hole in the exact center of the bottom of the bowl using a hot needle or a pin.
4.  Float the bowl on the water in the basin and immediately start a stopwatch.
5.  Watch as water slowly enters the bowl through the hole. Record the exact time it takes for the bowl to fill and sink entirely.
6.  **Engineering iteration:** If it sank in 5 minutes, your hole is too big, or your bowl is too small! Try a larger bowl or a smaller hole to increase the time. Keep iterating until you hit the magic ancient Indian measurement of exactly 24 minutes (One *Ghati*!).

## Project 2: The Pulse Pendulum

**The Challenge:**
Design an activity for measuring the pulse rate of your friends (beats per minute). Think of an activity where you can use your pulse to measure time, just like Galileo did!

**How to do it:**
1.  Teach your friend how to find their pulse on their wrist or neck. Have them count the beats for 60 seconds to find their resting heart rate (e.g., 75 beats per minute).
2.  Set up a simple pendulum. 
3.  Instead of a stopwatch, have your friend use their pulse! Tell them to count exactly 30 heartbeats while watching the pendulum swing, and count how many oscillations the pendulum completes in that "time".
4.  If their heart rate is steady, 30 beats represents a fixed amount of time (e.g., $\frac{30}{75} = 0.4$ minutes, or 24 seconds). You have successfully used biology to measure physics!

## Project 3: Playground Physics

**The Challenge:**
Visit a playground with a few swings. The swing is essentially a giant simple pendulum!

1.  **Does mass matter?** Sit on the swing (don't pump your legs) and have a friend pull you back and release you. Use a stopwatch to measure the time taken for 10 full swings. Divide by 10 to find the time period. Now, have a much heavier (or lighter) friend do the exact same thing. Does the time period change? *(Spoiler: According to the laws of physics, it shouldn't change noticeably!)*
2.  **Does length matter?** If the playground has swings with chains of different lengths (e.g., a baby swing vs. a tall kid swing), measure the time period of both. You will definitively prove that longer chains result in a slower swing (a longer time period)!
