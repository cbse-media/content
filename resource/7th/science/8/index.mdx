## Introduction

Have you ever wondered how people measured time before digital watches and smartphones existed? Long ago, humans relied on the rhythms of nature—the rising sun, the phases of the moon, and the changing seasons—to keep track of time. As civilizations advanced, the need to measure smaller, precise intervals of time became critical, leading to incredible inventions like water clocks, hourglasses, and eventually, the pendulum clock.

In this chapter, we will explore the fascinating history of timekeeping, the physics behind the simple pendulum, and how to mathematically describe how fast an object is moving by calculating its **speed**.

<Callout variant="info">
**Did you know?**
The *Ghatika-yantra* was an ancient Indian water clock consisting of a sinking bowl with a fine hole at the bottom. It took exactly 24 minutes to fill and sink, marking a unit of time called a *ghati*. A full 24-hour day was divided into exactly 60 *ghatis*!
</Callout>

## Formula Sheet: Speed, Distance, and Time

The core mathematical relationship in this chapter connects Speed, Distance, and Time. Use this triangle to remember the formulas:

*   **Speed** $=$ $\frac{\text{Total Distance}}{\text{Total Time}}$
*   **Distance** $=$ $\text{Speed} \times \text{Time}$
*   **Time** $=$ $\frac{\text{Total Distance}}{\text{Speed}}$

### Important Units
*   **SI Unit of Time:** Second ($\text{s}$)
*   **SI Unit of Speed:** Metres per second ($\text{m/s}$)
*   **Common Unit of Speed:** Kilometres per hour ($\text{km/h}$)

<Callout variant="tip">
**Quick Conversion Trick:**
*   To convert $\text{km/h}$ to $\text{m/s}$, multiply by $\frac{5}{18}$.
*   To convert $\text{m/s}$ to $\text{km/h}$, multiply by $\frac{18}{5}$.
</Callout>
