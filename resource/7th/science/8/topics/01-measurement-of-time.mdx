## Ancient Timekeeping

Long before the invention of mechanical or digital clocks, humans relied on the predictable cycles of nature to measure time. The cycle of the Sun rising and setting defined a **day**, while the phases of the Moon helped track **months**.

To measure smaller intervals of time within a single day, ancient civilizations devised clever instruments:

### 1. Sundials
A sundial determines time by tracking the changing position of the shadow cast by an object under the Sun's light. 
*   **The Jantar Mantar:** In Jaipur, India, the *Samrat Yantra* is the world's largest stone sundial. Built around 300 years ago, it is 27 meters tall! Its shadow moves at about 1 millimeter per second, allowing it to measure time intervals as short as 2 seconds.

### 2. Water Clocks (Clepsydra)
Water clocks used the steady flow of water to measure time. There were two main types:
*   **Outflow type:** A vessel with a hole at the bottom. The falling water level inside the vessel indicated the passing of time based on internal markings.
*   **Sinking bowl type (*Ghatika-yantra*):** A bowl with a tiny hole was placed in a larger basin of water. It slowly filled with water and eventually sank. The time it took to sink represented a specific unit of time (e.g., 24 minutes).

### 3. Hourglasses
An hourglass measures time using the steady flow of fine sand falling from an upper glass bulb into a lower one through a narrow neck.

### 4. Candle Clocks
Candles were manufactured to burn at a consistent rate. Markings on the side of the candle indicated how much time had passed as the wax melted away.

<Callout variant="info">
**The Shift to Mechanical Clocks**
As human civilization advanced and long-distance travel became common, highly accurate time measurement became critical for navigation. This led to the development of mechanical clocks driven by weights and gears in the 14th century, and eventually the revolutionary **pendulum clock** in the 17th century.
