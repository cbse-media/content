## Linear Motion

When an object moves along a straight line, its movement is called **linear motion**. However, *how* it moves along that straight line can vary.

### 1. Uniform Linear Motion

<Callout variant="tip">
An object moving along a straight line with a **constant (unchanging) speed** is said to be in **uniform linear motion**.
</Callout>

In uniform motion, the object covers exactly equal distances in equal intervals of time. 
*Example:* A car cruising on a perfectly empty, straight highway with the cruise control locked at $80 \text{ km/h}$. Every single hour, it will travel exactly $80 \text{ km}$. Every 30 minutes, it will travel exactly $40 \text{ km}$.

### 2. Non-Uniform Linear Motion

<Callout variant="tip">
If the speed of an object moving along a straight line **keeps changing** (speeding up or slowing down), it is said to be in **non-uniform linear motion**.
</Callout>

In non-uniform motion, the object covers unequal distances in equal intervals of time.
*Example:* A car driving through heavy city traffic. In the first 10 minutes, it might move $5 \text{ km}$. In the next 10 minutes, stuck at a red light, it might move $0 \text{ km}$. In the next 10 minutes, it might speed up to cover $10 \text{ km}$.

## Measuring Speed in Vehicles

How do we know the speed of a car at any exact moment? 

Modern vehicles are equipped with specific dashboard instruments:
1.  **Speedometer:** This dial (or digital readout) measures and displays the vehicle's instantaneous speed, usually in $\text{km/h}$. If the needle is jumping around, the car is in non-uniform motion!
2.  **Odometer:** This is a small meter that records the total distance the vehicle has traveled over its lifetime, measured in kilometers.
