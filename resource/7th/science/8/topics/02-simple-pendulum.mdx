## What is a Simple Pendulum?

A simple pendulum consists of a small metallic ball—called the **bob**—suspended from a rigid support by a long, weightless thread.

### The Mechanics of Oscillation
When the bob is resting straight down, it is in its **mean position**. If you pull the bob slightly to one side and release it, it begins to swing back and forth. This to-and-fro movement is an **oscillatory motion**. It is also a **periodic motion** because it repeats its path after a fixed, identical interval of time.

### Defining One Oscillation

The pendulum completes **one oscillation** when:
1.  The bob starts from the mean position ($O$), moves to the extreme left ($B$), swings to the extreme right ($A$), and finally returns to the center ($O$).
2.  *Alternatively*, you can measure it from extreme to extreme: starting at $A$, swinging to $B$, and returning back to $A$.

<Callout variant="tip">
**Definition:**
The time taken by the pendulum to complete exactly one full oscillation is called its **time period**.
</Callout>

## The Genius of Galileo

In the 16th century, the famous scientist Galileo Galilei noticed a swinging lamp in a church. Using his own pulse as a stopwatch, he realized something incredible: no matter how wide the lamp swung, it always took the exact same amount of time to swing back and forth!

Through experimentation, scientists discovered the fundamental rules of the pendulum:
1.  **Mass doesn't matter:** The time period does *not* depend on how heavy the bob is.
2.  **Length matters:** The time period depends *only* on the length of the string. A longer string makes a slower pendulum.

Because a pendulum of a specific length will always have the exact same, constant time period, it became the perfect mechanism to build highly accurate **pendulum clocks** (invented by Christiaan Huygens in 1656).

## The SI Unit of Time

In modern science, the standard **SI unit of time is the second (s)**.
Larger units are built from the second:
*   $60 \text{ seconds (s)} = 1 \text{ minute (min)}$
*   $60 \text{ minutes (min)} = 1 \text{ hour (h)}$
