## Project 1: Life Without Electricity

**Scenario:** Suppose that due to a severe storm, the power supply is disrupted in your area for two full days. List out which actions from your daily life you would not be able to do.

**Think about:**
*   **Lighting:** How will you see at night once the sun goes down? (No ceiling lights, tube lights).
*   **Heating and Cooling:** Can you run the fan or the air conditioner? How will you get hot water if you use an electric geyser?
*   **Kitchen:** What appliances will stop working? (Refrigerator, microwave, mixer grinder, electric induction stove). What will happen to the food in the fridge?
*   **Entertainment & Communication:** You cannot watch TV. Your mobile phone will eventually run out of battery and you won't be able to charge it. The Wi-Fi router will be off.
*   **Water:** If your house relies on an electric water pump to fill an overhead tank, you might run out of running water!

This thought experiment highlights just how dependent modern society is on a continuous supply of electricity.

## Project 2: Conducting a Home Audit

Prepare a list of objects in your home under three categories:

### 1. Objects which are electrical insulators only
*Look for items made entirely of plastic, wood, glass, or rubber.*
*   Wooden chairs
*   Plastic water bottles
*   Glass windows
*   Ceramic mugs
*   Cotton bedsheets

### 2. Objects which are electrical conductors only
*Look for items made entirely of metal.*
*   Stainless steel spoons and forks
*   Copper vessels
*   Iron nails
*   Aluminium foil
*   Keys

### 3. Objects made of BOTH (Insulators and Conductors)
*Look for electrical devices. They need conductors to work, but insulators to keep us safe!*
*   **Electrical Cords:** Copper wire inside (conductor), rubber coating outside (insulator).
*   **Screwdriver:** Steel shaft (conductor), plastic/rubber handle (insulator).
*   **Frying Pan:** Metal pan body (conductor for heat, but also conducts electricity), wooden or bakelite handle (insulator).
*   **Wall Switch:** Metal contacts inside the wall (conductor), plastic casing that you touch (insulator).

<Callout variant="tip">
**Why is this important?**
Understanding which parts of a tool are conductive and which are insulated is the key to electrical safety!
</Callout>
