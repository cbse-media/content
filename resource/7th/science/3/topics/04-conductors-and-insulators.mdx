## Testing Materials

If we replace the metal wires in a circuit with a piece of cotton thread or a rubber band, the lamp will not glow. Why? Because electric current cannot pass through all materials.

We can build a "conduction tester" by leaving a gap between two wires in a working circuit. By placing different objects across the gap, we can test their properties.

### Conductors

Materials through which electric current can flow easily are called **good conductors**, or simply **conductors of electricity**.

*   **Examples:** Metal spoons, coins, keys, safety pins, aluminium foil, pencil lead (graphite).
*   Metals like silver, copper, and gold are excellent conductors. We use **copper** for most electrical wires because it is highly conductive, relatively cheap, and abundant.
*   **Important:** Our human body is also a conductor of electricity!

### Insulators

Materials through which electric current cannot pass are called **insulators**, or **poor conductors of electricity**.

*   **Examples:** Wood, plastic scale, glass bangle, paper, rubber eraser, wax, ceramic.

## Why We Need Both

Both conductors and insulators are absolutely essential for using electricity safely and effectively.

1.  **Conductors** are used to make the internal parts of wires, the internal contacts of switches, and the metal prongs of plugs. They carry the electricity to where it needs to go.
2.  **Insulators** are used to cover the wires, form the outer casing of switches, and make the handles of electrical tools. They keep the electricity contained and prevent it from flowing into our bodies.

<Callout variant="warning">
**Safety Warning:**
Because our bodies conduct electricity, touching a live wire can cause severe injury or death. This is why you must never touch electrical switches or plugs with wet hands (water helps conduct electricity!), and never use equipment with damaged plastic insulation.
</Callout>
