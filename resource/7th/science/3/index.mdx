## Introduction

Electricity powers almost everything around us! From the lights in our homes and the fans that cool us down, to the mobile phones we use to communicate. In this chapter, we will explore the fundamental components that make up electrical devices. 

We will start by examining a simple torchlight to understand how portable electricity works. You will learn how to build your own simple electrical circuits, understand the difference between conductors and insulators, and learn how to draw circuit diagrams using standard scientific symbols.

<Callout variant="warning">
**Safety First!**
Never perform experiments with the power supply from wall sockets at your home or school. The electricity from poles and wall sockets is very high voltage and can be extremely dangerous. For all our experiments, we will only use small, safe **electric cells (batteries)**.
</Callout>

## Quick Reference: Circuit Symbols

When drawing circuits, we use standard symbols instead of drawing the actual objects. This makes it easier for scientists and engineers worldwide to understand the diagrams.

| Component | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Electric Cell** | A long line (positive terminal) and a short, thicker line (negative terminal). |
| **Battery** | A combination of two or more cell symbols connected end-to-end. |
| **Switch (ON)** | Two dots connected by a straight line (the circuit is closed). |
| **Switch (OFF)** | Two dots with the line lifted up (the circuit is open). |
| **Electric Lamp** | A circle with an 'X' or a curved filament inside. |
| **LED** | A triangle pointing at a line, with two small arrows pointing outward (showing light emitting). |
| **Wire** | A simple straight line. |
