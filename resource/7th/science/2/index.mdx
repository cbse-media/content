## Introduction

Have you ever wondered why a lemon tastes so sour, while soap feels slippery to the touch? Or why a yellow turmeric stain on a white shirt suddenly turns red when you wash it with soap? The answer lies in the chemical nature of these substances!

In this chapter, we will explore the colourful world of substances by classifying them into three main categories: **Acidic**, **Basic**, and **Neutral**. We will learn how to act like scientists by using special substances called **indicators** to test the nature of everyday items found in our kitchens and bathrooms.

<Callout variant="info">
**Did you know?**
You don't always need a fancy laboratory to do chemistry! Nature provides us with many natural indicators like red cabbage, turmeric, and even the petals of a red rose to help us identify acids and bases.
</Callout>

## Key Concepts and Definitions

*   **Acidic Substances:** Substances that are sour to taste and turn blue litmus paper red. (e.g., Lemon juice, Vinegar).
*   **Basic Substances:** Substances that feel soapy/slippery to touch, are bitter to taste, and turn red litmus paper blue. (e.g., Soap solution, Baking soda).
*   **Neutral Substances:** Substances that are neither acidic nor basic. They do not change the colour of indicators. (e.g., Pure water, Sugar solution).
*   **Indicators:** Special substances that change colour (or odour) when added to acidic or basic solutions.
*   **Neutralisation:** A chemical reaction where an acid and a base mix to cancel each other out, forming salt, water, and heat.
