## Project 1: The Colored Shadow Experiment

**The Setup:** Repeat the shadow formation activity (shining a torch at an opaque object to cast a shadow on a wall). But this time, cover the face of the torch tightly with a piece of colored transparent paper (like red cellophane).

**The Question:** What color will the shadow be? 

**The Conclusion:**
The shadow will still be **black / dark**! 
A shadow is simply the *absence* of light. Even if the light source is shining bright red light, the opaque object still blocks that red light from hitting the wall. The dark patch behind the object is just an area where no light is reaching. Therefore, changing the color of the light source (or the color of the object) does not change the color of the shadow itself.

*(Note: The wall around the shadow will appear red because the red light is hitting it!)*

## Project 2: The Infinity Mirror

**The Setup:** A single plane mirror forms a single image. What happens if you place two mirrors next to each other at an angle, or perfectly parallel to each other? Place a small object (like a coin or a colorful bottle cap) between them.

**What happens?**
*   **Angled Mirrors:** If you place two mirrors at a 90-degree angle, you won't just see 1 reflection, you will see **3 reflections**! The mirrors reflect the object, and then they reflect *each other's* reflections! If you make the angle sharper (like 60 degrees), you will see even more reflections.
*   **Parallel Mirrors:** If you stand two mirrors up so they are facing each other perfectly parallel, and place the object between them, you will see an **infinite tunnel of reflections**! The image bounces back and forth between the two mirrors forever, getting slightly fainter each time.

## Project 3: The Giant Virtual Tree

**The Setup:** You are given a small piece of a plane mirror (the size of a pocket mirror). 
**The Question:** Can this tiny piece of mirror form an image of an object much larger than the mirror, like a giant oak tree?

**Think and Predict:**
Most people think a small mirror can only show a small object. But think about how a mirror works: it just changes the direction of light entering your eye.

**Carry out the activity:**
Stand far back from a large tree. Hold the tiny mirror up close to your eye and angle it towards the tree behind you. 

**The Result:** 
**Yes!** You can easily see the entire massive tree reflected inside the tiny mirror. Because the tree is far away, the light rays from the very top and the very bottom of the tree converge to fit into the small surface area of the mirror before reflecting into your eye. The mirror acts like a window into a virtual world behind it.
