## Sources of Light

We cannot see anything in complete darkness. We need a source of light to make objects visible.

### Luminous vs. Non-Luminous Objects

*   **Luminous Objects:** Objects that generate and emit their own light. 
    *   *Natural Sources:* The Sun, stars, lightning, and certain animals like fireflies.
    *   *Artificial Sources:* Light bulbs, candles, torches, and LEDs.
*   **Non-Luminous Objects:** Objects that do not emit their own light. We only see them because they reflect the light from luminous objects into our eyes.
    *   *Examples:* The Moon (it reflects sunlight!), a table, a book, your own body.

<Callout variant="tip">
**Why use LEDs?**
Light Emitting Diode (LED) lamps are modern artificial light sources that consume much less power, are brighter, and last far longer than traditional incandescent bulbs. They are highly energy-efficient and better for the environment!
</Callout>

## How Materials Interact with Light

When a beam of light hits an object, the material determines what happens next.

1.  **Transparent Materials:** These materials allow light to pass through them almost completely. You can see clearly through them. (Example: Clear glass window, clean water).
2.  **Translucent Materials:** These materials allow only a portion of light to pass through them. The light gets scattered, so you cannot see clearly through them. (Example: Tracing paper, frosted glass).
3.  **Opaque Materials:** These materials block light completely. No light passes through. (Example: Cardboard, a wooden door, a brick wall).

*Experiment Check:* If you shine a torch at an opaque piece of cardboard, you will not see the spot of light on the wall behind it. Instead, you will see a dark patch—a shadow!
