## The Journey of Food

Animals consume complex food components like carbohydrates, proteins, and fats. These must be broken down into simpler forms before the body can use them. This breakdown occurs in a long tube called the **alimentary canal**.

The alimentary canal consists of several parts:
`Mouth $\rightarrow$ Oesophagus (Food Pipe) $\rightarrow$ Stomach $\rightarrow$ Small Intestine $\rightarrow$ Large Intestine $\rightarrow$ Rectum $\rightarrow$ Anus`

### 1. The Mouth Cavity
The journey begins here. Teeth break down the food through crushing and chewing (**mechanical digestion**). 
Simultaneously, the salivary glands secrete **saliva**. Saliva moistens the food and contains digestive juices that break down complex carbohydrates (starch) into simple sugars. *This is why chewing a plain piece of chapati or rice for a long time eventually makes it taste sweet!*

### 2. The Food Pipe (Oesophagus)
The tongue pushes the chewed, softened food into the oesophagus. The walls of this tube gently contract and relax in a wave-like motion to push the food downwards into the stomach.

### 3. The Stomach
The stomach is a muscular bag that churns the food. Its inner lining secretes:
*   **Digestive Juices:** To break down proteins.
*   **Acid (Hydrochloric Acid):** Kills harmful bacteria entering with the food and helps the digestive juices work.
*   **Mucus:** Protects the stomach lining from being damaged by its own acid.
The food turns into a semi-liquid mass here.

### 4. The Small Intestine
This is where the final digestion and nutrient absorption happen! The small intestine receives digestive secretions from three sources:
1.  Its own inner lining.
2.  **The Liver:** Secretes **bile**, which neutralizes stomach acid and breaks down fats into tiny droplets.
3.  **The Pancreas:** Secretes pancreatic juice to break down carbohydrates, proteins, and fats.

**Absorption:** The inner lining of the small intestine is covered in thousands of tiny, finger-like projections. These increase the surface area, allowing the digested nutrients to pass efficiently into the blood vessels. The blood then carries these nutrients to all parts of the body.

### 5. The Large Intestine & Egestion
The undigested, unabsorbed food moves into the large intestine (which is shorter but wider than the small intestine). Here, water and salts are absorbed, turning the waste into a semi-solid called **stool**. 

The stool is stored in the **rectum** and eventually expelled through the **anus** in a process known as **egestion**.

<Callout variant="tip">
**Healthy Digestion:**
Eating fibre-rich foods (fruits, vegetables, whole grains) helps the large intestine function properly, making stool easier to pass. The large intestine also contains helpful bacteria that break down fibre and produce essential nutrients!
</Callout>
